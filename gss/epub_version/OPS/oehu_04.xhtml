Scene 3, Behind The Political Curtain

F (Finckster): Sorry to keep you waiting.

D (Damien): [Rises from sitting in a folding chair.] Thank you for your time Congressman Finckster.

F: Come into my office and pardon the mess. I keep hearing promises ... that someone will clean this all up. Alas, it was hit by a fresh tomato. I mean tornado.

D: I knew politics was messy.

F: While I'm looking for bugs, would you like some cold pizza?

D: Not if that's where the bugs have been marchin' in ranks. 'N the cheeze looks more counterfeit than a spray-on tan. So no thanks.

F: Oh not those kinds of bugs. Us politicians need to keep our secrets unknown [looking to each side] from political spies. In fact, turn off your phone.

D: [Turns off smartphone.] Uh sure.

F: Jordon told me to trust you with behind-the-curtain and under-the-trap-door kinds of magic that go on in politics.

D: That's what I'd really like to know.

F: Let's start with what you probably think you know. There are names for the left like liberals, environmentalists, and workers. And there are names for the right, such as conservatives, capitalists, and, lately, racists. But those are just distractions. Entertaining distractions. They're useful to distract from the big conflict.

D: What's bigger than the conflict between left 'n right?

F: Allow me to introduce you to a new way to look at politics.

D: My brain's empty 'n ready to be filled.

F: The much bigger conflict is between the political up and the political down.

D: Political up? Political down? What the heck?

F: The political up, that's where most of the voters are.

D: Voters of which party?

F: Both parties.

D: Huh?

F: Ah, you too have failed to notice that both parties are controlled by people with money.

D: That part I knew.

F: The biggest campaign contributors are in the political down.

D: Should we be callin' out fat people?

F: Not that kind of biggest. They give the biggest amounts of money. They provide the money source that lifts us politicians high in the polls. Not enough and we get dashed against the rocks during a primary.

D: You mean rich folks, right?

F: Of course they're all rich. But some of the rich are in the political up.

D: Pardon me. I'm as confused as if I just stepped out of a high-capacity industrial laundry washer.

F: Just like most voters. That's what the political down wants. That's why they avoid being named.

D: But among themselves they call themselves the political down?

F: No, that's just how I think of them.

D: Please go on, this is blindingly illuminatin'.

F: Of course the political down prefers our party, the Republican party.

D: I see that.

F: But also the political down has infiltrated the Democratic party.

D: Wait a moment. Why would the political down give money to both parties? That's as stupid as bettin' on both horses in a two-horse race.

F: Ah, here's a big secret. They only assist Democratic candidates during primary elections. Of course I'm simplifying hugely.

D: Ah, they use money to influence who wins each Democratic primary election. But in the general election they just support the Republican candidates?

F: Brilliant, isn't it? More dazzling than a smiling model in an auto ad.

D: Now I'm beginning to see why we, er, most voters, dislike both candidates, the Republican and the Democrat. Nothin' personal, of course.

F: Us politicians know we're stuck in the middle. Between voters and our biggest campaign contributors. So no offence taken. Hopefully you now see why voters are distracted by the entertaining conflict between left and right. It's the modern-day equivalent of ancient Roman rulers who distracted plebians with bloody fighting among slaves in their immense Colosseum. Except that modern-day plebians get to vote which slave, er, puppet, wins the staged fight.

D: With their clout, what's the political down really care about?

F: Profits for the businesses they own. What especially delights them are tax breaks for their businesses. And when they can get a legal monopoly they cling to it like a drowning man clings to a floating piece of styrofoam.

D: Lower taxes 'n no competition. Wow, what a sweet deal! It sounds as though their lobbying efforts amount to corruption. Am I wrong?

F: You're right.

D: So I'm confused. The Republican party seems to care about some non-business issues such as abortions, and gay marriage, Christian values, immigration, 'n such, right?

F: They do put those things on the flag they fly. But taxes, and ideally subsidies and monopolies, are their real priorities. Of the party insiders. 'N they get their marching orders from the biggest campaign contributors.

D: Now I think I'm seein' why neither political party really gives a damn 'bout protecting coral reefs, fixin' climate change, givin' homeless folks an assist, or listenin' to what dolphins have to say. The down folks use religious 'n racial issues to divide 'n distract.

F: Here's another division tactic. Money can fund extreme liberal agendas. Like when the LGB category got extended. To include queers and transgenders. That frightens many to the Republican side.

D: Ah, lots'a voters fall for the idea that the enemy of their enemy is their friend.

F: That's a powerful divide and conquer tactic.

D: About those monopolies. 'Course I know I can't start a business that competes with one of theirs. What else should I know?

F: Indeed, you can't start an insurance company, can't design a bridge without an engineering degree, and can't offer health advice as a smiling host on TV.

D: Those make sense.

F: You also can't earn much money selling the herb stevia as a sweetener for drinks because laws block stevia from appearing on grocery shelves next to sugar or next to artificial sweeteners.

D: So stevia's a natural sweetener. But it can't be sold where it would cut into profits for sugar companies. 'N makers of artificial sweeteners?

F: Yup. And speaking of sugar, long ago Congress passed laws against buying sugar that's grown outside the U.S.

D: Is that so?

F: Here in the states we pay sometimes almost twice the price compared to other nations.

D: Coffee'd taste sweeter without that price hike.

F: And it's easy to hide the giving of funds to both sides. In fact there are two brothers in Florida in the sugar biz. One brother is a Republican who gives buckets of money to Republican politicians. The other brother is a Democrat who gives buckets of money to Democratic politicians. Ain't that sweet?

D: 'N as hard to follow as a rat chased by a cat. So they don't give a damn 'bout left or right? They just lobby for sugar ...

F: subsidies. And the canals that enable Florida farmers to grow sugarcane. But the biggest profits come from converting sugarcane into sugar, and that's what's done by the businesses those bros own.

D: That's a sour deal for us. Why isn't this more obvious?

F: Commodity price lists used to list sugar and U.S. sugar. Now they're named sugar number eleven and sugar number sixteen.

D: That's a slick trick. But I'm confused. Don't some rich folks prefer the Democratic party?

F: Lots of lawyers get lots of their business from Democratic voters, so they like Democratic politicians. And then there are industries that subdivide into left and right.

D: Huh? I thought I was followin' you down main street, but you just ducked into a dark alley.

F: Banks prefer the Republican party. 'N credit unions prefer the Democratic party.

D: Wow! I think I now see who's in the political down. 'N why they oppose us, the voters, in the political up. But how do so very few rich folks outvote us, the non-rich, who outnumber them? Like hairs outnumber whiskers on a walrus.

F: There's something in politics called vote splitting.

D: What do spitting goats have to do with politics?

F: Not goats. Votes. As in voting.

D: Vote spitting?

F: Not spitting, although long ago there was lots of spitting in politics. It's splitting, vote splitting, and it's the election version of divide and conquer.

D: Ah, vote splitting.

F: That's it!

D: 'N divide 'n conquer. Isn't that the battle tactic armies use when they separate enemy soldiers from one another? 'N then they can attack the smaller groups one at a time?

F: Yes, and in fact, voting is rooted in war and fighting. That's why women didn't vote. They weren't counted because they didn't fight. Of course those counting experts never met my brother's ex-wife. Anyway, the counting was done to figure out how many men would be in each gang. And then the smallest gangs negotiate with one another and join forces. Otherwise they get slaughtered.

D: So the winner's the side that joins the most gangs into just one very big army?

F: Yes, and the remaining gangs join together into one opposition army. So it comes down to two armies. And that's where size matters.

D: Ah, so long ago votin' began as a way to count potential army sizes. 'N the smaller army surrenders?

F: Saves a lot of dying, no? And when splitting can't be done physically, then splitting psychologically works too. In politics, that's what religious differences are used for.

D: So how does this vote splitting work?

F: Well, it involves math.

D: Oh dear, ya mean, mathematics?

F: Indeed.

D: In my opinion math's the devil's favorite form of torture. If I had to choose between doin' just one page of math homework, or standin' under a tall fir tree gettin' cut down, I'd be there yellin' timber!

F: I don't like it either. And I don't understand vote splitting well enough to clearly explain how it all works. I just know that it works. So we, the politicians, are powerless to fight it.

D: Ya said there's distractions. How can just distractions hide this vote splittin' so effectively?

F: You've heard of the electoral college? And gerrymandering?

D: I admit long ago I wondered if Jerry Mander was someone important. Now I realize those're 'bout the fight between Republicans against Democrats.

F: That's why news reporters are not blocked from writing and speaking about those distracting topics of unfairness.

D: Which now I know helps distract from, 'n hide, the bigger fight between political up 'n political down.

F: And you've heard of the money bonus that a third-party presidential candidate can get if they get more than a certain percent of the vote?

D: Aha! That's a tactic to split votes away from ... well who?

F: Third parties are almost always on the left. At least in the U.S. Whenever needed they're quietly seeded with funds from conservatives. That splits votes away from the Democratic nominee.

D: I see. It just affects the distractin' balance between left 'n right.

F: And if a conservative third-party candidate begins to do well in the polls, they get a bribe to drop out. That concentrates conservative voters' ballots on the single Republican nominee.

D: 'N now I see why news media 'n those slippery political ads don't have as much influence as many folks assume. The splittin' of votes among candidates durin' primaries, that's their key to controllin' you politicians. Like puppets, er, well, like I'm not sure what.

F: That's not what I like to hear, yet between you and me, you're right.

D: 'N yet we have social media, so surely some bright wits are callin' attention to these dirty politics.

F: To deal with that the political down hires critics to write blogs, post memes, and tweet photoshopped photos that support absurd conspiracy theories. Plus they hire coders to make bots that upvote their propaganda, and downvote the truth.

D: I'd wondered why some people online attack with such anger. So it's partly 'cause they're gettin' paid to be a faker?

F: Yup. Mister Mischievious from Michigan is getting paid to inspire Furious Fiona from Florida to re-post the clever meme he created to trash Hillary Clinton. And you should know attack ads also work. Because lots of voters get swayed by believing that expression the enemy of my enemy is my friend.

D: That's stupid. Don't they see that just means they're shootin' at the same target?

F: And getting hit by friendly fire! Ah yes, stupidity is easy to exploit. And it's more common than ants at a picnic.

D: I think I'm figurin' this out. It's not like I expected. Yet it makes too much sense to be fiction.

F: There is one bit of history worth knowing.

D: Oh I hate history. Crammin' more facts 'bout the Alamo 'n the Civil War into my head might crowd out what I've learned.

F: This is simple. Back when primary elections seemed harmless, vote splitting was obvious. That's because each political party offered two or even three candidates. But if one big party offered just one candidate, that solo one won.

D: Ah, 'cause voters in another party would split their votes among their similar candidates.

F: So the winning strategy came to be for each party to have their own primary election. The idea was to discover which politician deserves the solo spot. In what we now call the general election.

D: Ah! Now the splittin's hidden in primaries where the winner's never from the wrong party.

F: Indeed! You learn fast! So here's a test.

D: Oh dear lord! I hate tests! 'N this might be 'bout history no less.

F: Let go of that foolish fear. This answer should be clear.

D: [Sighs.] 'Kay

F: What helped Barack Obama win the two thousand eight Democratic primary election against Hillary Clinton?

D: Based on what you've taught, some Republicans may have thought, ... that well, ...

F: You're on the right track.

D: ... thought to give money to Obama to defeat their bigger enemy, Clinton.

F: Correct so far.

D: 'N they probably made the assumption that a black man couldn't win the general election. Is that right?

F: You did it! That's right! Although of course we cannot trace all donations. But that's sure to have been a part of what transpired.

D: The Republicans who gave contributions to Obama must've been dumbfounded. Having the president be an African American.

F: And that gives you an example of a tactic that wouldn't work if vote splitting were not such a useful gimmick for rigging elections.

D: Huh?

F: If unfair vote splitting disappeared, then that blocking kind of tactic wouldn't work. Because in the general election, each party would offer two or three candidates per party.

D: So what can be done to make the evil split not happen?

F: I really don't know, and don't care. The political stress was making a mess of my ulcers. Giving them their own ulcers. So I retired. That's why I can tell you about this atrocity. But remember you didn't learn any of this from me. If you go to newspaper reporters with these insights I'll shine light on the skeletons in the closet of your friend Jordon. He asked me to trust you.

D: I won't be a snitch or fink. 'N anyway I can't yet link the problem of vote splittin' to a solution. One that can defeat this unfairness. So I need to learn more. Do you know who would know how to fix this mess? The unfair politics.

F: Probably that would be a professor of mathematics.

D: Oh dear, advanced mental gymnastics! Just the thought of math scares me so!

F: Well I've got to go. I wish you luck in understanding yet more so maybe someday you can help clean up the muck in the arena called politics. In the meantime, the political world is upside down. Voters allow the rich to be at the top. Yes, politicians have all been bought. But as voters you've got more power than the X-Men and Justice League and a swarm of locusts on the same team.

D: So you think maybe someday the voters will reign supreme?

F: Eventually. Hopefully. Then us politicians will become your puppets. But currently we're the puppets of wealthy business owners.

D: I'm seein' a bit of hope for a fix. From dysfunctional politics. The solution, whatever it might be, I hope it comes quickly.

F: Good luck! But remember, you didn't hear any of this from me.

D: Thank you Congressman for clearing up the mess in my brain. It's good to know I haven't yet gone insane. You've tilted my head to better see the political terrain. Now I know the greedy rich distract us with left versus right. Yet up versus down, that's the real fight.
