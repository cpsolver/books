Scene 3, Behind The Political Curtain

Action: Damien is sitting in folding chair, then stands when Congressman Finckhster enters.

Finckhster: Sorry to keep you waiting.

Damien: Thank you for your time Congressman Finckhster.

Finckhster: Come into my office. Please pardon the mess.

Damien: I know that politics is messy.

Finckhster: While I'm looking for bugs, would you like some cold pizza?

Damien: The cheeze looks fake, so no thanks.

Finckhster: I always have to look for bugs. There are those who would love to learn what we'll say. Speaking of which, please turn off your phone.

Action: Damien turns off smartphone.

Damien: Oh sure.

Finckhster: Matt told me to trust you with secrets about how politics really works.

Damien: That's what I'd really like to know.

Finckhster: Let's start with what you probably think you know. There are names for the left like liberals, progressives, workers, and more. And there are names for the right, such as conservatives, Christians, Wall Street, and more. But those are just distractions. Entertaining distractions. They are useful to distract from the big conflict.

Damien: What's bigger than the conflict between left and right?

Finckhster: Allow me to introduce you to a new way to look at politics.

Damien: My brain is empty and ready to be filled.

Finckhster: The much bigger conflict is between the political up and the political down.

Damien: Political up? Political down?

Finckhster: The political up, that's where most of the voters are.

Damien: Voters of which political party?

Finckhster: Both parties.

Damien: How can that be?

Finckhster: Ah, you too have failed to notice that both parties are controlled by people with money.

Damien: That part I knew.

Finckhster: The political down is where the biggest election campaign contributions come from.

Damien: You mean rich folks, right?

Finckhster: Of course they are all rich, but some rich people are in the political up.

Damien: Pardon me, I'm lost.

Finckhster: Just like most of the voters. That's what the political down wants. That is why they avoid being named.

Damien: But among themselves they call themselves the political down?

Finckhster: No, that's just how I think of them.

Damien: Please go on, this is so illuminating.

Finckhster: Of course the political down prefers our party, the Republican party.

Damien: I see that.

Finckhster: But also the political down has infiltrated the Democratic party.

Damien: Wait a moment. Why would the political down give money to both parties? That's a waste.

Finckhster: Ah, here's a big secret. They only care about the Democratic primary elections.

Damien: Ah, they use money to influence who wins each Democratic primary [extend "i" sound] election. But in the general election they just support the Republican candidates?

Finckhster: Brilliant, isn't it?

Damien: Now I am beginning to see why we, er, most voters, dislike both candidates, the Republican and the Democrat. Nothing personal, of course.

Finckhster: We know that as politicians we are stuck in the middle, between voters and our biggest campaign contributors, so no offence taken. You are now seeing why the voters are distracted by the entertaining conflict between left and right?

Damien: I think I see. But please tell me, what does the political down care about?

Finckhster: Profits for the businesses they own. Especially, what they really, really, really, want are taxes to be lower for their businesses. And when they can get a legal monopoly they will cling to it with everything they've got.

Damien: Because it brings so much money. It sounds as though their lobbying efforts amount to corruption. Am I wrong?

Finckhster: You are right.

Damien: So I'm confused. The Republican party seems to care about some non-business issues such as abortions, and gay marriage, Christian values, immigration, and such, right?

Finckhster: Not really. Of course they say those things matter. And they are minor annoyances. But those are ignored when things such as taxes and monopolies are at stake. Moral issues serve as entertaining distractions between left and right. They take away attention from up and down.

Damien: Now I think I'm seeing why neither political party really gives a damn about helping starving people, protecting rare animals and the environment, climate change, and such. Those issues are used by the down folks to divide and to distract the voters.

Finckhster: Plus sometimes money is used to fund extreme liberal causes. Like when the LBG category got extended to include queer and transgender folks. That frightens lots of voters to the Republican party.

Damien: Ah voters who fall for the idea that the enemy of their enemy is their friend.

Finckhster: It's a divide and conquer tactic.

Damien: What else should I know about monopolies? I know that's why I cannot start a business that competes with one of theirs.

Finckhster: Indeed, you can't start a bank, can't start an insurance company, can't practice law without a law degree, can't offer health advice without a medical degree.

Damien: Those make sense.

Finckhster: You also can't earn much money selling the herb stevia as a sweetener for drinks because laws block stevia from appearing on grocery shelves next to sugar or next to artificial sweeteners.

Damien: So stevia is a natural sweetener, but can't be sold where it would cut into profits for sugar companies and the makers of artificial sweeteners?

Finckhster: Yup, and speaking of sugar, long ago Congress passed laws against buying sugar that's grown outside the U.S.

Damien: Is that so?

Finckhster: The result is that here in the states we pay twice as much as the rest of the world.

Damien: So without that monopolistic law, we would pay half the price?

Finckhster: Indeed, these legal monopolies force consumers to buy from the businesses that are owned by people in the political down.

Damien: And those in the political down give money to the candidates in both parties?

Finckhster: In fact there are two brothers in Florida in the sugar biz. One brother is a Republican, the other a Democrat. They each give lots of money to politicians in "their" party.

Damien: So they don't give a damn about left or right? They just lobby for sugar ...

Finckhster: subsidies. And the canals that help the Florida farmers grow sugar cane. But the biggest profits come from converting sugar cane into sugar, and that's what's done by the businesses those brothers own.

Damien: This is making lots of sense. And yet there are some rich folks who seem to prefer the Democratic party.

Finckhster: Lots of lawyers get lots of their business from Democratic voters, so they like Democratic politicians. And then there are industries that subdivide into left and right.

Damien: Huh?

Finckhster: Banks prefer the Republican party, while credit unions prefer the Democratic party.

Damien: Wow! I think I now see who's in the political down, and why they oppose us, the voters, in the political up. But how do so very few rich folks outvote us, the non-rich, who greatly outnumber them?

Finckhster: There's something in politics called vote splitting.

Damien: What do spitting goats have to do with politics?

Finckhster: Not goats. Votes. As in voting.

Damien: Vote spitting?

Finckhster: Not spitting, although long ago there was lots of spitting in politics. It's splitting, vote splitting, and it's the election version of divide and conquer.

Damien: Ah, vote splitting.

Finckhster: That's it!

Damien: And divide and conquer, isn't that the battle tactic armies use when they separate soldiers or ships or whatever from one another? And then they can attack the much smaller groups one at a time?

Finckhster: Yes, and in fact, politics is rooted in war and fighting. That's why women didn't vote. They weren't counted because they didn't fight. The counting was done to figure out how many soldiers would be in each group. And then the smallest groups negotiate with one another and join forces. Otherwise they get slaughtered.

Damien: So the winner was the side that joined the most groups into just one big group?

Finckhster: Yes, and the remaining groups had to join into one opposition group. So it came down to two groups. And counting determined who outnumbered who.

Damien: In reverse, the best tactic was to split up the enemy army, right?

Finckhster: Indeed, and when the splitting can't be done physically, then splitting psychologically works too.

Damien: Ah, so long ago voting just served to count the potential army sizes. And the smaller army surrendered?

Finckhster: Saves a lot of dying, no?

Damien: So how does this vote splitting work with votes?

Finckhster: Well, it involves math.

Damien: Oh dear, you mean, mathematics?

Finckhster: Indeed.

Damien: I hate math.

Finckhster: I don't like it either. And I don't understand vote splitting well enough to clearly explain how it all works. I just know that it works. So we, the politicians, are powerless to fight it.

Damien: You said there are distractions. How can just distractions hide this vote splitting so effectively?

Finckhster: You've heard of the electoral college? And gerrymandering?

Damien: I've heard lots about those unfair things.

Finckhster: They serve to focus voters on the fight between left and right. That's why news reporters are not blocked from writing and speaking about those topics of unfairness.

Damien: Which I now know helps to hide, distract from, the real fight between the political up and the political down.

Finckhster: And you've heard of the money bonus that a third-party presidential candidate can get if they get more than a certain percent of the vote?

Damien: Aha! That's a tactic to split votes away from ... well who?

Finckhster: Third parties are always on the left. Sometimes they are quietly given funds by people on the right, who want to split votes away from the Democratic nominee.

Damien: I see. It just affects that distracting balance between left and right. And now I am realizing why I rarely see a conservative third party. I suppose any conservative third-party candidates get offered money to drop out so they don't split votes away from the Republican nominee?

Finckhster: You've got it!

Damien: And now I see why news media and those awful political ads don't have as much influence as many people think. The splitting of votes among candidates in the primary elections of both parties, that's their key to controlling you politicians like puppets, er, well, like I'm not sure what.

Finckhster: That's not what I like to hear, yet between you and me, you're right.

Damien: And yet we have social media, so surely some folks are calling attention to these dirty political tactics.

Finckhster: Ah, but they get lots of downvotes and criticism from a few folks who are happy to get paid to write comments, blogs, websites, and such stuff. And they are backed by bots that are rented by the organizations that serve the political down.

Damien: I had wondered why some people online attack with such anger. It's partly (be)cause they are getting paid for that as a job?

Finckhster: Yup. And you should know attack ads do work because lots of voters get swayed by believing that expression "the enemy of my enemy is my friend".

Damien: That's stupid. Don't they see that just means they are shooting at the same target?

Finckhster: And getting hit by friendly fire! Ah yes, stupidity is easy to exploit.

Damien: Finally I'm figuring this out. What I've learned is not what I expected it to be about.

Finckhster: There is one bit of history worth knowing.

Damien: Oh I hate history.

Finckhster: This is simple. Vote splitting used to be obvious in the U.S. because often there were two or maybe even three candidates from the same political party. So voters who liked that party split their votes among the similar candidates. In that case the winning strategy was for a party to have only one candidate from that party.

Damien: Just one candidate per party. That was a winning strategy?

Finckhster: Yes, if the other parties offered more than just one candidate.

Damien: I think I'm getting this.

Finckhster: Good, but don't tell anyone else what I'm telling you.

Damien: Of course not.

Finckhster: So voters who liked the party with just one candidate automatically concentrated their votes on just that one candidate, causing that candidate to win. That's what led to creating what we call primary elections. And now each party nominates just a single candidate.

Damien: That's a useful bit of history I didn't know. It explains why each party offers just one nominee. And I suppose vote splitting is why we have just two political parties?

Finckhster: Indeed! You learn fast! So here's a test.

Damien: Oh dear Lord! I hate tests! And this might be about history no less.

Finckhster: Let go of that foolish fear. This will be pretty easy.

Damien: [sighs] OK

Finckhster: What helped Barack Obama win the two thousand eight Democratic primary election against Hillary Clinton?

Damien: Based on what you've taught me, Republicans were involved.

Finckhster: Right, er left, er, correct, so far.

Damien: So people in the political down must have given money to Obama to defeat their bigger enemy, Hillary.

Finckhster: Correct so far.

Damien: And they probably thought, believed, assumed, that a black man could not win the general election. Is that right?

Finckhster: You did it! That's right! Although of course we cannot trace all the money, but that's sure to have been a part of what happened.

Damien: Those folks must have been dumbfounded when he won.

Finckhster: And that provides an example of a tactic that wouldn't work if vote splitting were not a problem.

Damien: Huh?

Finckhster: If the unfair vote splitting were solved, there could be two or three candidates per party in the general election. Then that blocking kind of tactic would not work.

Damien: So what can be done to fix this mess?

Finckhster: I really don't know, and don't care. I'm retiring at the end of my term. That's why I can tell you this stuff. But remember you didn't learn any of this from me. If you go to newspaper reporters with these insights I will shine some light on the skeletons in the closet of your friend Matt who asked me to trust you.

Damien: I won't be a snitch or fink. And anyway I don't yet know how the problem of vote splitting can be solved. So I need to learn some more. Who would know how vote splitting can be solved?

Finckhster: Probably that would be a professor of mathematics.

Damien: Oh dear, just the thought of math scares me so!

Finckhster: Well I've got to go. I wish you luck in understanding yet more so maybe someday you can help clean up this political mess. In the meantime, the political world is upside down. Voters put the rich at the top. Yes, politicians have all been bought. But as voters you have got more power than you realize.

Damien: So you think maybe this can be solved?

Finckhster: Eventually. Hopefully, someday voters will be at the top. And us politicians will become your puppets. But until vote splitting is solved we are puppets of wealthy business owners.

Damien: It can't come soon enough. Again, thank you so much for your time Congressman Finckhster.

Finckhster: Good luck, but always remember, you didn't hear any of this from me.
