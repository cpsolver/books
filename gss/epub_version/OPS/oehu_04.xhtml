Scene 3, Behind The Political Curtain

Action: Damien is sitting in a folding chair, then stands when Congressman Finckster enters.

Finckster: Sorry to keep you waiting.

Damien: Thank you for your time Congressman Finckster.

Finckster: Come into my office. Please pardon the mess. I keep hearing promises that someone will clean this all up, but, alas, it was hit by a fresh tornado.

Damien: I knew that politics was messy.

Finckster: While I'm looking for bugs, would you like some cold pizza?

Damien: Not if that's where the bugs have been. Anyway, the cheeze looks more counterfeit than a spray-on tan. So no thanks.

Finckster: Oh not those kinds of bugs. There are spies in the world of politics, and they'd love to hear what I'll say. Speaking of which, please turn off your phone.

Damien: [turns off smartphone] Oh sure.

Finckster: Jordon told me to trust you with behind-the-curtain and under-the-trap-door kinds of magic that go on in politics.

Damien: That's what I'd really like to know.

Finckster: Let's start with what you probably think you know. There are names for the left like liberals, environmentalists, and workers. And there are names for the right, such as conservatives, capitalists, and, lately, racists. But those are just distractions. Entertaining distractions. They're useful to distract from the big conflict.

Damien: What's bigger than the conflict between left and right?

Finckster: Allow me to introduce you to a new way to look at politics.

Damien: My brain is empty and ready to be filled.

Finckster: The much bigger conflict is between the political up and the political down.

Damien: Political up? Political down? What the heck?

Finckster: The political up, that's where most of the voters are.

Damien: Voters of which political party?

Finckster: Both parties.

Damien: How can that be?

Finckster: Ah, you too have failed to notice that both parties are controlled by people with money.

Damien: That part I knew.

Finckster: The political down is where the biggest election campaign contributions come from. The money source that lifts us high in the polls, or dashes us onto the rocks in a primary election.

Damien: You mean rich folks, right?

Finckster: Of course they're all rich, but some rich people are in the political up.

Damien: Pardon me, I'm as confused as if I just stepped out of a high-capacity industrial laundry washer.

Finckster: Just like most voters. That's what the political down wants. That's why they avoid being named.

Damien: But among themselves they call themselves the political down?

Finckster: No, that's just how I think of them.

Damien: Please go on, this is blindingly illuminating.

Finckster: Of course the political down prefers our party, the Republican party.

Damien: I see that.

Finckster: But also the political down has infiltrated the Democratic party.

Damien: Wait a moment. Why would the political down give money to both parties? That's as stupid as betting on both horses in a two-horse race.

Finckster: Ah, here's a big secret. They only assist Democratic candidates during primary elections.

Damien: Ah, they use money to influence who wins each Democratic primary election. But in the general election they just support the Republican candidates?

Finckster: Brilliant, isn't it? More dazzling than a smiling model in an auto ad. Of course I'm simplifying a bit.

Damien: Now I'm beginning to see why we, er, most voters, dislike both candidates, the Republican and the Democrat. Nothing personal, of course.

Finckster: We know that as politicians we're stuck in the middle, between voters and our biggest campaign contributors, so no offence taken. Hopefully you now see why voters are distracted by the entertaining conflict between left and right. It's the modern-day equivalent of ancient Roman rulers who distracted plebians with bloody fighting among slaves in their immense Colosseum. Except that modern-day plebians get to vote which slave, er, puppet, wins the staged fight.

Damien: With their clout, what does the political down really care about?

Finckster: Profits for the businesses they own. What especially delights them are tax breaks for their businesses. And when they can get a legal monopoly they cling to it like a drowning man clings to a floating tree branch.

Damien: Lower taxes and no competition. Wow, what a sweet deal. It sounds as though their lobbying efforts amount to corruption. Am I wrong?

Finckster: You are right.

Damien: So I'm confused. The Republican party seems to care about some non-business issues such as abortions, and gay marriage, Christian values, immigration, and such, right?

Finckster: They do put those things on the flag they fly. But taxes, and ideally subsidies and monopolies, are their real priorities.

Damien: Now I think I'm seeing why neither political party really gives a damn about protecting coral reefs, fixing climate change, giving homeless folks an assist, or listening to what dolphins have to say. The down folks use religious issues to divide and distract.

Finckster: Here's another division tactic. Money can fund extreme liberal agendas. Like when the LGB category got extended to include queers and transgenders. That frightens many to the Republican side.

Damien: Ah voters who fall for the idea that the enemy of their enemy is their friend.

Finckster: That's a powerful divide and conquer tactic.

Damien: About monopolies, of course I know I can't start a business that competes with one of theirs. What else should I know?

Finckster: Indeed, you can't start an insurance company, can't practice law without a law degree, and can't offer health advice as a smiling host on TV.

Damien: Those make sense.

Finckster: You also can't earn much money selling the herb stevia as a sweetener for drinks because laws block stevia from appearing on grocery shelves next to sugar or next to artificial sweeteners.

Damien: So stevia is a natural sweetener, but can't be sold where it would cut into profits for sugar companies and the makers of artificial sweeteners?

Finckster: Yup, and speaking of sugar, long ago Congress passed laws against buying sugar that's grown outside the U.S.

Damien: Is that so?

Finckster: Here in the states we pay almost twice the price compared to other nations.

Damien: Coffee would taste a bit sweeter without that price hike.

Finckster: And it's easy to hide the giving of funds to both sides. In fact there are two brothers in Florida in the sugar biz. One brother is a Republican who gives buckets of money to Republican politicians. The other brother is a Democrat who gives buckets of money to Democratic politicians. Ain't that sweet?

Damien: And as hard to follow as a rat chased by a cat. So they don't give a damn about left or right? They just lobby for sugar ...

Finckster: subsidies. And the canals that enable Florida farmers to grow sugar cane. But the biggest profits come from converting sugar cane into sugar, and that's what's done by the businesses those bros own.

Damien: That's a sour deal for us. But don't some rich folks prefer the Democratic party?

Finckster: Lots of lawyers get lots of their business from Democratic voters, so they like Democratic politicians. And then there are industries that subdivide into left and right.

Damien: Huh? I thought I was following you down main street, but you just ducked into a dark alley.

Finckster: Banks prefer the Republican party, while credit unions prefer the Democratic party.

Damien: Wow! I think I now see who's in the political down, and why they oppose us, the voters, in the political up. But how do so very few rich folks outvote us, the non-rich, who outnumber them? Like hairs outnumber whiskers on a walrus.

Finckster: There's something in politics called vote splitting.

Damien: What do spitting goats have to do with politics?

Finckster: Not goats. Votes. As in voting.

Damien: Vote spitting?

Finckster: Not spitting, although long ago there was lots of spitting in politics. It's splitting, vote splitting, and it's the election version of divide and conquer.

Damien: Ah, vote splitting.

Finckster: That's it!

Damien: And divide and conquer, isn't that the battle tactic armies use when they separate soldiers from one another? And then they can attack the smaller groups one at a time?

Finckster: Yes, and in fact, voting is rooted in war and fighting. That's why women didn't vote. They weren't counted because they didn't fight. Of course those counting experts never met my brother's ex-wife. Anyway, the counting was done to figure out how many soldiers would be in each gang. And then the smallest gangs negotiate with one another and join forces. Otherwise they get slaughtered.

Damien: So the winner is the side that joins the most gangs into just one very big army?

Finckster: Yes, and the remaining gangs join together into one opposition army. So it comes down to two armies. And that's where size matters.

Damien: Ah, so long ago voting began as a way to count potential army sizes. And the smaller army surrenders?

Finckster: Saves a lot of dying, no? And when splitting can't be done physically, then splitting psychologically works too. In politics, that's what religious differences are used for.

Damien: So how does this vote splitting work?

Finckster: Well, it involves math.

Damien: Oh dear, you mean, mathematics?

Finckster: Indeed.

Damien: In my opinion math's the devil's favorite form of torture. If I had to choose between doing just one page of math homework, or standing under a big fir tree getting cut down, I'd be there yelling timber!

Finckster: I don't like it either. And I don't understand vote splitting well enough to clearly explain how it all works. I just know that it works. So we, the politicians, are powerless to fight it.

Damien: You said there are distractions. How can just distractions hide this vote splitting so effectively?

Finckster: You've heard of the electoral college? And gerrymandering?

Damien: I admit long ago I wondered if Jerry Mander was someone important. Now I realize those are about the fight between Republicans versus Democrats.

Finckster: That's why news reporters are not blocked from writing and speaking about those distracting topics of unfairness.

Damien: Which now I know helps to distract from, and hide, the real fight between the political up and the political down.

Finckster: And you've heard of the money bonus that a third-party presidential candidate can get if they get more than a certain percent of the vote?

Damien: Aha! That's a tactic to split votes away from ... well who?

Finckster: Third parties are almost always on the left. At least in the US. Whenever needed they're quietly seeded with funds from conservatives. That splits votes away from the Democratic nominee.

Damien: I see. It just affects that distracting balance between left and right.

Finckster: And if a conservative third-party candidate begins to do well in the polls, they get a bribe to drop out. That concentrates conservative voters' ballots on the single Republican nominee.

Damien: And now I see why news media and those slippery political ads don't have as much influence as many people assume. The splitting of votes among candidates during primaries, that's their key to controlling you politicians like puppets, er, well, like I'm not sure what.

Finckster: That's not what I like to hear, yet between you and me, you're right.

Damien: And yet we have social media, so surely some bright wits are calling attention to these dirty politics.

Finckster: To deal with that the political down hires critics to write blogs, post memes, and tweet photoshopped photos that support absurd conspiracy theories. [refine later] Plus they hire coders to make bots that upvote their propaganda, and downvote the truth.

Damien: I had wondered why some people online attack with such anger. It's partly 'cause they're getting paid for that as a job?

Finckster: Yup. Mister Mischievious from Michigan is getting paid to inspire Furious Fiona from Florida to re-post the clever meme he created to trash Hillary Clinton. And you should know attack ads also work. Because lots of voters get swayed by believing that expression the enemy of my enemy is my friend.

Damien: That's stupid. Don't they see that just means they're shooting at the same target?

Finckster: And getting hit by friendly fire! Ah yes, stupidity is easy to exploit. And it's more common than ants at a picnic.

Damien: I think I'm figuring this out. It's not like I expected.

Finckster: There is one bit of history worth knowing.

Damien: Oh I hate history. Cramming more facts about the Alamo and the Civil War into my head might crowd out what I've learned.

Finckster: This is simple. Back before primary elections became normal, vote splitting was obvious. That's because each political party offered two or even three candidates. But if one big party offered just one candidate, that solo one won.

Damien: Ah, 'cause voters in other parties split their votes among their similar candidates.

Finckster: So the winning strategy came to be for each party to have their own primary election to discover which politician deserved the solo spot in what we now call the general election.

Damien: Ah! Now the splitting is hidden in primaries where the winner is never from the wrong party.

Finckster: Indeed! You learn fast! So here's a test.

Damien: Oh dear lord! I hate tests! And this might be about history no less.

Finckster: Let go of that foolish fear. This answer should be clear.

Damien: [sighs] OK

Finckster: What helped Barack Obama win the two thousand eight Democratic primary election against Hillary Clinton?

Damien: Based on what you've taught me, Republicans were involved.

Finckster: Right, er left, er, correct, so far.

Damien: So people in the political down must have given money to Obama to defeat their bigger enemy, Clinton.

Finckster: Correct so far.

Damien: And they probably thought, believed, assumed, that a black man couldn't win the general election. Is that right?

Finckster: You did it! That's right! Although of course we cannot trace all the money, but that's sure to have been a part of what happened.

Damien: Those folks must have been dumbfounded when the winner was an African American.

Finckster: And that provides an example of a tactic that wouldn't work if vote splitting were not a problem.

Damien: Huh?

Finckster: If the unfair vote splitting were solved, there could be two or three candidates per party in the general election. Then that blocking kind of tactic would not work.

Damien: So what can be done to fix this mess?

Finckster: I really don't know, and don't care. The political stress was giving my ulcers their own ulcers, so I retired. That's why I can tell you this stuff. But remember you didn't learn any of this from me. If you go to newspaper reporters with these insights I will shine some light on the skeletons in the closet of your friend Jordon who asked me to trust you.

Damien: I won't be a snitch or fink. And anyway I can't yet link the problem of vote splitting to a solution. So I need to learn some more. Who would know how vote splitting can be solved?

Finckster: Probably that would be a professor of mathematics.

Damien: Oh dear, just the thought of math scares me so!

Finckster: Well I've got to go. I wish you luck in understanding yet more so maybe someday you can help clean up this political mess. In the meantime, the political world is upside down. Voters allow the rich to be at the top. Yes, politicians have all been bought. But as voters you've got more power than the X-Men and Justice League and a swarm of locusts put together.

Damien: So you think maybe this can be solved?

Finckster: Eventually. Hopefully. Someday voters will get to the top. And us politicians will become your puppets. But until vote splitting is solved we are puppets of wealthy business owners.

Damien: I'm seeing a bit of hope for a fix from mischievious politics. The solution, whatever it should be, I hope it comes quickly.

Finckster: Good luck! But remember, you didn't hear any of this from me.

Damien: Thank you Congressman for clearing up the mess in my brain. It's good to know I haven't yet gone insane. You've tilted my head to better see the political terrain. Now I know the the greedy rich distract us with left versus right. Yet up versus down, that's the real fight.
