Scene 2, Money Madness

Damien: [Enters room while Jordon remains seated.] 'ello mister Morton. Grafton, m' fiance's father, said you make money dance. 'N I'd like to advance m' state of finance. Could ya teach me some tricks?

Jordon: Shall we start with some licks? In the fridge is some mint chocolate chip ice cream. Want some?

Damien: Tha's too rich a taste for me. Risin' out of serfdom. That's my dream. I wanna pass to th' middle class. I long t' be financially secure as an entrepreneur.

Jordon: I applaud your taste for money. Lots of folks are fond of stuffed bears, warm and fuzzy. But a wallet that's fat, just like me, what's better than that?

Damien: Withou' money my world feels upside down, like a turtle on it's back.

Jordon: With money, my world feels right side up. I've got a knack for earning lots of wealth. It's like getting payouts spilling onto the floor from a slot machine. Yet I want more. I can't afford my own jet, or a yacht. Not there yet. Don't yet got all I want.

Damien: S' what's th' trick? Of gettin' money to fund a life not wrecked.

Jordon: You've got to treat other people with respect, before you step on their toes. Then anything goes. That's the trick. That's how I behave like a ... an entrepreneur.

Damien: S' you put Benjamins before humans?

Jordon: Think about this. The love of money is built into our language. We have zillions of words for money. There's alimony, annuities, assets, bonds, and bonus, bounty, bribe, and capital, cash, and commodities, credit, currency, debit, discount, expense, and funds, income, interest, investment, options, pension, ransom, rebate, and remittance, rent, revenue, securities, shares, subsidy, and wage, and even those awful words like charity and donation.

Damien: 'N besides words for mula and dough, there're words like banker, accountant, miser, 'n vulture capitalist.

Jordon: And investor, and venture capitalist, which are what I am.

Damien: Yeah, money's damn importan'.

Jordon: Did you know that long ago, when writing was new, writing was used for banking 'n accounting?

Damien: Huh?

Jordon: That way money was stored in banks, not in pockets nor in bags anymore. That protected traders from pirates, thieves, and marauding desperados.

Damien: Ah, pirates. Tha' was a profession back when swashbuckling exams were open to th' poor.

Jordon: Pickpockets, pirates, and highway robbers are becoming as uncommon as blue-footed boobies. Now that money travels out of sight at the speed of light.

Damien: 'N th' modern version of pirates is lawyers?

Jordon: Oh no, today's lawyers are like ancient scribes.

Damien: S' how do you choose where to put th' money you've got, to get more?

Jordon: Besides side-stepping traps set by gold diggers, I only invest in high-profit deals.

Damien: Why not invest in businesses tha' offer value?

Jordon: The high-profit one has to compensate for the other eight, the deals that flop. Like Enron, America Online, and the early pets dot com.

Damien: S' you're seekin' th' one big standout you can brag about?

Jordon: Ah, yes, the unicorn. The one that swells my pockets til they burst. Missing out on one of those would be the worst. Yes, one unicorn in the forest is worth more than five zebras in the brush.

Damien: Zebras?

Jordon: Those are the small businesses that grow slow, by making and selling something of quality that consumers readily want. They don't need our venture capital for big marketing blitzes.

Damien: Isn't th' whole business scene about selling what customers want? 'R crave, like caffeine, nicotine, 'n Beanie Babies [chocolate covered almonds?]?

Jordon: Sure money can be used to undercut obstinate customer dissatisfaction.

Damien: What I mean is ideally I'd like to find fundin' for a business idea. I've invented a tool for cleanin' leaf gutters, withou' gettin' on a ladder. Would ya invest in a business that sells th' best kind of tool for that task?

Jordon: Nah, that's not much better than a cordless bungee-jumping franchise.

Damien: Lots o' people get injured, fallin' off a ladder while cleanin' gutters. Some land on their head, and end up dead.

Jordon: That's a great spin! Fear of falling. That's a fear that would sell lots of product. Do you have a patent?

Damien: I can't afford that cost. Tha's like thousands of dollars.

Jordon: Many tens of thousands of clams.

Damien: I couldn't afford even a few hundred dollars to ...

Jordon: Why would I invest unless there's a barrier against competition? There's gotta be a monopoly. Or some kind of virtual monopoly. That way my investment doesn't evaporate like a raindrop on a Detroit skyscraper's corner office window on a dry summer day.

Damien: So a workin' prototype's not enough?

Jordon: Nope. That's as useless as a machine-gun mount on the back of a golf cart. What the biz needs is a big amount of income, plus customers and testimonials. Then we'd invest.

Damien: If income I already had, I could self-fund it. 'N with that I could go to a bank 'n ask for a loan.

Jordon: Ha, the bank officials might nod ernestly. They might even encourage you to fill out forms. But when you've stepped out the door, they'll wet themselves with laughter.

Damien: If I were t' buy a house they'd loan for that.

Jordon: A mortgage, hah. It doesn't work like that. Instead the bank buys the house. Your down payment's big enough to cover a university education. So if you default after a few months, they sell the house, and you've bought a cap and gown and diploma for a banker's kid.

Damien: So banks don't loan money?

Jordon: They still do a bit, sometimes. Mostly now they're high-rolling gamblers that make casinos look like kids playing a game of Monopoly. They make big money by buying and selling, ah, another word for money, derivatives. Big things, not small things.

Damien: Wha' are derivatives?

Jordon: Those are contracts on paper, or the cyberspace equivalent, written by lawyers. Lots of times they're promises to pay money if something happens, like if interest rates rise or fall.

Damien: That sounds a lot like gamblin'.

Jordon: It is. And gambling, that's what we venture capitalists do too. We loan money. It's like those ancient sea voyages. Someone had to buy the ship and pay sailors before they could even set sail. Then, on return, if they returned, the cargo was sold, and the backer got his share of the profits.

Damien: 'N th' captain 'n crew got what?

Jordon: What they got was a temporary pass to the middle class. Until they got swindled, or beat at roulette, or divorced.

Damien: Bu' th' backer got more?

Jordon: Of course, 'cause he took the biggest risk.

Damien: Those times sound barbaric.

Jordon: They were. In those ancient times fighting was done by soldiers, and hard labor was done by workers. Now fighting is so much safer. It's done by lawyers fighting with legal documents instead of swords, and wearing colorful neckties instead of armour or bullet-proof vests. Whoever pays the most wins in court 'n gets the most. Quite civilized nowadays.

Damien: I think I see how money works. So how can I switch from bein' 'n employee to become an employer? Ownin' a small business, that's my dream. Some success, financially, so I c'n buy a fixer-upper house, pay for ultra-fast access to, er, ..., kitten videos, 'n drive a car that didn't come off Henry Ford's production line. Not a yacht, a cabin no, I jus' want fair rewards for my hard work. How do I take that leap?

Jordon: Leverage is the key. You need a vision. And you must find workers who follow you like ducklings follow their mom. You work them hard. You hire lawyers to sue any competitors. And of course you hire marketing folks who super-size your PR blitzes that get consumers to trust your brand.

Damien: S' I should start a cult? 'N of course there'd be 'n app with that.

Jordon: That might put you on the wealthy map. If you shift the idolotry to money.

Damien: 'T sounds like makin' money is about lyin' and cheatin'.

Jordon: It's not lying. That's not cheating. It's marketing and stepping on toes. In fact anything goes. But not lying and cheating. 'Cause that lands you in the slammer. That'd be a bummer 'cause your bank accounts can't protect you when you drop the bar of soap in the prison shower.

Damien: T' get ahead financially I have to step on people's toes?

Jordon: Or a gentle push to the nose. Business is all about competition. It's the very foundation of a free market.

Damien: Stomping on toes. Anything goes. That's th' trick of corporations. Why are they th' winners in th' business world?

Jordon: Ah the key is politics. We shareholders and investors give money to politicians. Then to us they listen. That's not cheating. It's not against any law. I share mula with any politician who passes laws that dump big amounts of golden coins into my bank accounts.

Damien: After your money buys you a louder voice, in politics, wha'dya get from th' politicians you buy?

Jordon: Tax breaks and subsidies. And sometimes legal monopolies. Oh I do love these. With these breaks, my business thrives.

Damien: But not so for others' lives. Th' customers. Th' employees.

Jordon: If someone else gets beaten, that's their choice. They need to speak up with a louder voice.

Damien: Don't you fear th' folks who want to tax th' wealthy?

Jordon: The business that I closely own, with more profit it could buy a Tesla SUV, or a ski lodge, or a yacht. Something I don't yet got. My biz buddies and I would share it. That tactic evades hefty income tax rates. So an extra tax on the rich, it misses me.

Damien: So th' key is ownin' a biz?

Jordon: Or getting on the board of directors. Three businesses pay me to travel to their board meetings. There we vote to reduce labor costs. Using bigger profits from the previous year's decisions, we vote for huge bonuses for ourselves. That's sandwiched within a resort vacation. At a fancy spot where I lounge for days with a date who's hot. Or at least I indulge in rich meals with a Flirty Felicity from Philly. Plus I do some side deals. And most of it's deductible, a money word that brings me joy.

Damien: So th' tax breaks and subsidies are for companies. Th' ones you own, or own shares of?

Jordon: Yup. The paperwork's a hassle, but those sly tricks are legal. All because we pay off politicians.

Damien: Don't lawyers also assist?

Jordon: Yes our lawyers fight battles in court. They exterminate our enemies. But nowadays no blood is shed. The loser only suffers financially.

Damien: It seems th' lawyers at th' top of th' legal food chain always win. Why's that?

Jordon: They write the bills that get passed into law. So of course they know where the loopholes are. It's all a big family. I do love it.

Damien: Is that all that money's needed for, to get those competitive edges?

Jordon: A way to cling to our riches is to own newspapers and TV stations.

Damien: Why that?

Jordon: So when there's dirty linen in a biz we own it doesn't become known.

Damien: How 'bout news sources you don't own?

Jordon: The business buys advertising spaces from those places. Have you noticed that scandalous stories about grocery stores never appear in newspapers? That's because grocery stores pay for lots of newspaper ads.

Damien: That protection racket's not what a small store can afford to play.

Jordon: My dear boy, there's no money to be made by aiming low. It's big businesses that earn the dough. The really big ones are chains that use mass media to broadcast their ads across a big city, or even a state.

Damien: I know someone who wants to start a restaurant. Can't he buy ads for just a neighborhood?

Jordon: Only costly printed ads cover small neighborhoods. Well, 'til recently, when NextDoor and News Here Now came along.

Damien: Are there other tricks?

Jordon: Doing tricks keeps a corporation as busy as an escort in Las Vegas during convention season. Another trick is big corporations hire the big four accounting firms to get around laws that were co-written by those big four accounting firms.

Damien: Tha's, er, a sweet deal for corporations. Are there yet other tricks?

Jordon: As many as an eagle has feathers. The most important trick is to give political campaign contributions to political candidates who, after the election, pass laws that give our businesses tax break after tax break. That's why big businesses pay six-figure salaries so freely.

Damien: Ah yes, money in politics. I know 'bout that. Bu' I don't yet understand how it works. Non-rich voters greatly outnumber th' wealthy, so how can dinero outweigh those votes?

Jordon: We've infiltrated both parties. This is how we control who wins the primary elections of both parties. So general elections are between a puppet Republican and a puppet Democrat.

Damien: Yeah, puppets win. I knew tha' part. But I hadn't noticed th' infiltration tactic.

Jordon: Republican voters assume Democrats are dimwits, and Democratic voters assume Republicans are dimwits. In the primary, the winner's always from the correct party, so the money bias goes unchallenged. In the general election it's too late. For added insurance in a tight race we might give a bit of money to the one we hate. But we give far more to the one we favor. That way we can't lose.

Damien: Why don't news reporters expose how 't works?

Jordon: That flow of money is harder to follow than a mouse running through a cornfield. Such as when rich wives register as Democrats and give family money to conservative Democratic politicians during primary elections.

Damien: There's somethin' else going on isn't there?

Jordon: You are bright! Yes, we pay news sources to entertain and distract. The biggest of distractions are those endless debates about issues of religion.

Damien: Religion?

Jordon: Abortion is the really useful one. The Republican party squeezes that for all it's worth.

Damien: So th' Republican party doesn't really care 'bout abortion?

Jordon: We sorta care, but not as much as tax breaks and subsidies and our legal monopolies.

Damien: I 'ave to admit voters don't see through that. They're so distracted by th' fight between left 'n right.

Jordon: They are suckers. They believe the enemy of their enemy is their friend.

Damien: Even I know that's stupid. So, how do ya know which candidates to fund, 'n by how much?

Jordon: The consultants we hire tell us where the money should go.

Damien: 'N how do those consultants know which puppets, er, politicians to bribe, er, fund?

Jordon: They monopolize their secrets, their strategies. They're like astrologers and alchemists from long ago. Back before science tanked the profits from selling horoscopes, farmer's almanacs, and Doctor Dan's mighty healing elixer.

Jordon: Politicians know, right?

Jordon: I've never asked. It just works. If it aint broke, there's no need to fix its quirks, right?

Damien: I'm not dubious, but I'm curious. How's it work?

Jordon: Curiosity killed the cat.

Damien: Satisfaction brought him back. So perhaps is there a politician ya know who might be willin' to explain how it works — so I can earn more money your way? For my fiancé.

Jordon: I do know the recipient of lots of my dough. He recently retired from Congress. Maybe he knows how it works.

Damien: Is there somethin' I can do for you, if you can maybe arrange that?

Jordon: Indeed, you can. Come up with a better business plan. A unicorn I can brag about.

Damien: I'll try. 'N now I know what barriers t' jump over to get to my big dreams of financial success. You've taught me much. I confess I'm grateful t' learn your process.

Jordon: I'm happy to give advice to young hard-working men who adore dinero. Especially if you can help me earn more.

Damien: I'll do my best t' rise to th' very top. [Whispers.] 'N get to th' bottom of th' corruption. [Normal voice.] Thank you so much for taking time t' share your wisdom.

Jordon: You're welcome! And remember the trick.

Damien: Startin' with respect, then steppin' on toes, anything goes.
