Scene 4, Fighting Against Darkness

Action: Desha is sitting on a stone bench in a cemetary eating a cookie. Damien walks to face her.

Desha: You're way late.

Damien: Are you Desha?

Desha: Who else would be in this spot in this cemetary at this time?

Damien: Nice to meet you. I'm Damien.

Desha: Of course.

Damien: Thanks for meeting with me.

Desha: For nothing.

Damien: Huh?

Desha: That's the meaning of "de nada" in Spanish.

Damien: Yeah I know. My mom is Puerto Rican.

Desha: Then why did you ask?

Action: Damien rolls his eyes.

Damien: You said you wanted to meet with me.

Desha: Congressman Finckster said I should meet with you.

Damien: To discuss why politics is so dysfunctional, you said.

Desha: Damn right! It's so so so dysfunctional.

Damien: Did he tell you about vote splitting?

Desha: What do goats have to do with politics?

Damien: Not goats. Not spitting. Vote splitting.

Desha: Splitting votes in half? That's a half-wit idea.

Damien: Not splitting ballots. Splitting voters.

Desha: That's murder. Yes politicians do murder innocent people by their flawed laws.

Damien: That's not what vote splitting is.

Desha: Then it sounds quite esoteric. What's important is that too many people are suffering under corruption.

Damien: Yeah, corruption's so unfair. It's like leaving just four squares of toilet paper on a toilet paper roll.

Desha: It's millions of times worse. Corruption kills through economic suffering that leads to suicide, homicide, overdoses, homelessness, and more. Corruption kills by allowing gun makers to push assault rifles into the hands of mentally unbalanced young males. And corruption kills pedestrians. And the companion of corruption is despair. Life is so unfair.

Damien: Vote splitting's at the root of unfair elections, so do ya wanna hear about it?

Desha: Tell me how you think it makes a difference.

Damien: Here's a way to understand it. Suppose there are four politicians.

Desha: What are their names?

Damien: I don't know, this is just a generic example.

Desha: You describe them, I'll name them.

Damien: Two of them are puppets of wealthy business owners. The other two promise to reduce corruption, so they're more popular.

Desha: The names of the first two are Pinoke and Oscar.

Damien: Huh?

Desha: Pinoke is a nickname for Pinochio, the puppet. And Oscar's last name is the Grouch. He lives in trash just like corrupt politicians.

Damien: Ah, OK.

Desha: The other two are named Barack, 'cause we can hope for change, and Hypatia 'cause women should rule.

Damien: With these four candidates, who would win?

Desha: Either Barack or Hypatia, obviously, 'cause voters want less corruption. They don't want puppets, not Pinoke, not Oscar.

Damien: Right, the voters would get what they want, if money were not involved.

Desha: So, now you'll say that advertising hypnotizes the voters?

Damien: Only a few are that stupid.

Desha: So tell me why you think money changes who wins.

Damien: Wealthy business owners, who're the biggest campaign donors, in private they choose which puppet gets their money. If they choose Pinoke, then Oscar is told to drop out.

Desha: That would make Oscar a grouch. What if he should refuse?

Damien: They point out without their dough he'll lose. Or they bribe him with a favor.

Desha: A dumpster full of cookies?

Damien: Sure. That would leave Pinoke and Hypatia and Barack. Who would win that three-way race?

Desha: Ah, lots of voters would split their votes between Barack and Hypatia. That would make it easier for Pinoke to win.

Damien: Plus lots of money for ads that attack Hypatia and Barack, plus news coverage that favors Pinoke, that'd be enough to rig the race.

Desha: A puppet wins. That's unfair! More voters prefer either Barack or Hypatia.

Damien: That's what I've learned. But now I'm stuck. I don't know how to change elections so they don't suck.

Desha: We can call attention to this deception. We'll get voters to concentrate their votes on either Barack or Hypatia.

Damien: But who decides which candidate to abandon?

Desha: We the voters could get one to drop out, right?

Damien: There's another backup trick. The wealthiest campaign donors would give money to yet another candidate who also promises to reduce corruption.

Desha: Let's name this one Ghandi. Yes, that tactic's difficult to beat. It splits the voters even more, making it hard to defeat their single puppet.

Damien: That's why political parties nominate just one selection for the general [extend first syllable] election.

Desha: Now it makes even more sense why we have just two main political parties.

Damien: Yup. And years ago the puppet politicians passed a law that gives funding to a third-party presidential candidate who gets more than five percent of the vote.

Desha: I thought that was a good thing. Now I see it's a bribe that seduces liberals into splitting their votes. While conservatives quietly concentrate their votes on just one candidate.

Damien: You learn fast.

Desha: I have a degree in political science. But they never taught us this.

Damien: The unfairness is hidden. It's hidden by numbers, and math to me is scary.

Desha: What scares me is that our planet is losing snow leopards and elephants, and rhinocerous ... ... es, and dolphins, and corral reefs, and plant species that could cure and feed.

Damien: Is that what got you into politics?

Desha: At the age of fourteen, ... my mother died. And that's when lots of people, especially my father, lied. He and they said everything would turn out fine.

Damien: I can't imagine what it would be like to lose someone so close at such a young age.

Desha: I can't either. It's still unreal. Especially because just six years later my brother was killed in the Middle East.

Damien: How did you cope?

Desha: I almost lost hope. Then I realized my mother was killed by incompetent city officials. They failed to time pedestrian walk signals to give walkers a head start. Before the green light for cars. And I realized, that basically, my brother was defending profits for oil companies in the Middle East.

Damien: Is that why we're in this graveyard?

Desha: My mother and brother are buried over there.

Damien: Ah.

Desha: Over time I traced the local corruption to the state level, then to DC, then to Congress where they write laws so corrupt.

Damien: Yes, corruption. I wouldn't know how to define it, but I see it frequently.

Desha: It's lying, payoffs, graft, and fraud, misrepresenting, duplicity, betrayal, underhanded treachery, swindling, and deluding, unscrupulous behavior, deceitful cheating, palm greasing, and misleading, dishonesty, bribery, and devious embezzelment, entrapment, tax evasion, legal wickedness, coersion, and unethical monopolizing. Not to mention littering.

Damien: It's like an arrangement where someone can gain personal rewards by not caring about the suffering of others.

Desha: Yeah, that's the devil. And so I won't forget I've got a tatoo.

Damien: Forget what? Can I see the tatoo?

Desha: That's for private tours only.

Damien: What does it say?

Desha: When at last I realized the real causes of their deaths, I decided to kick some ... sense into the political world. The tatoo says "kick ass." So I've been fighting since.

Damien: Who're you fighting now?

Desha: I've hit another turning point. Originally I thought the enemy was capitalism, and that socialism was the opposite.

Damien: And it's not?

Desha: It's not that simple. Then I fought against the Republican party. But then I realized the Democratic party has been infiltrated by big-money donors.

Damien: I too just learned the Republican party is not the real enemy.

Desha: I want to figure out whose butt I should kick. But I'm stuck.

Damien: I think I've found the tipping point.

Desha: The place where a little effort can make a huge change?

Damien: Yeah, but that path's blocked.

Desha: By what?

Damien: By math. It's blocking me from figuring out how to turn the world rightside up.

Desha: I've had some math. What do you want to figure out?

Damien: How should votes be counted to get results that are fair?

Desha: I don't know. But I do know someone who might know the answer.

Damien: Can he teach us ...

Desha: Not he but she.

Damien: Who is she?

Desha: She's a math prof.

Damien: A professor of math?

Desha: Yes, at the state university. That's where I got my degree.

Damien: I couldn't cope with even community college. And textbooks don't work for me. I can't go where printed words try to take me.

Desha: So you're a loser.

Damien: Huh?

Desha: You're choosing to give up instead of fight. That's losing.

Damien: I'm not brainy. How can I fight against math?

Desha: What took you this far?

Damien: I'm short and not handsome. So working hard to earn money is all I've got to keep my fiance happy.

Desha: What's so special about her?

Damien: She appreciates me. And like I said, I'm not a popular guy.

Desha: All you need is one.

Damien: Do you have someone?

Desha: I have my Willie.

Damien: Pardon?

Desha: My partner's name is Willie.

Damien: Are you two happy?

Desha: What's your disability? Did you never learn to read? Do the words move around on the page? Do books put you to sleep? There has to be something more.

Damien: It hurts to read. The words are a pain to look at.

Desha: Have you tried color-tinted glasses?

Damien: Huh? I don't follow.

Desha: Of course not. Never mind. I'll send you to a website where you can find out yet more. In the meantime, do you want me to arrange a meeting with this math professor?

Damien: OK but only if she can explain things in spoken words and not use lots of jargon, or academic speak.

Desha: I think she can be OK with that. And maybe I can translate if that's what you really need.

Damien: It's what I need. What do you need?

Desha: In my early youth we were so happy then, even though money we lacked. Then the world turned upside down. I saw suffering that I hadn't seen before. And I traced the cause of suffering to corruption in government, especially Congress. Those ... grrr ... politicians are the puppets of greedy money huggers. Now I want to send them to hell, those greedy evil buggers.

Damien: Which ones? The politicians or the big campaign contributors?

Desha: Whoever is most to blame.

Damien: From what I can tell, they're both clueless about why money works so well, as puppet strings. Can we fight against cluelessness?

Desha: I'll fight against whoever's to blame. I call on the spirits of the dead here to inspire me to find a way to shine a light on corruption. I want a light that is so bright that even evil money huggers and egotistic puppets cannot fight back. Not with their wealth, not with lawyers, not through hired workers, and not with all they think they have. I know that light casts out darkness. In that light the path to better times will appear. Until that happens I'll keep on fighting. Fighting with words. Fighting with deeds. Doing whatever it takes to bring good times back to us, the voters, the majority.

Damien: Whatever it takes? Yes, it seems it will take some looking into mathematics.

Desha: I'm willing to help you fight against your fear of mathematics. Let's do this!

Damien: I'm ready to fight! I just hope there's no evil fractions. Otherwise I'm ready to find out how to turn the world right side up.
