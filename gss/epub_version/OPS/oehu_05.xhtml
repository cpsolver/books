Scene 4, Fighting Against Darkness

De (Desha): [Sitting on a stone bench in a cemetery eating a cookie.] You're way late. And don't call this a date.

D (Damien): Are you Desha?

De: Who else would be in this spot in this cemetery at this time?

D: Nice to meet ya. I'm Damien.

De: Of course.

D: Thanks for meetin' with me.

De: For nothing.

D: Huh?

De: That's the meaning of de nada in Spanish.

D: Yeah I know. My mom's Puerto Rican.

De: Then why did you ask?

D: Is there cannibis in that cookie?

De: No you can't have one.

D: I just wondered what's in it.

De: Chocolate chips, dried cherries, rolled oats, stevia, sugar, and ghee, but nothing funny.

D: Ya said you wanted to meet with me.

De: Congressman Finckster said I should meet with you.

D: To discuss why politics is so dysfunctional, you said.

De: Damn right! It's so so so dysfunctional.

D: So, did he tell ya about vote splittin'?

De: What do goats have to do with politics?

D: Not goats. Not spitting. Vote splitting.

De: Splitting votes in half? That's a half-wit idea.

D: Not splitting ballots. Splitting voters.

De: That's murder. Yes politicians do murder innocent people. They write laws that come from Thomas Crapper's brainchild.

D: That's not what vote splitting is.

De: Then it sounds quite esoteric. What's important is that too many people are suffering under cruel corruption.

D: Yeah, corruption's so unfair. It's like leavin' just four squares of toilet paper onna toilet paper roll.

De: It's zillions of times worse. Corruption kills through economic suffering that leads to suicide, homicide, overdoses, homelessness, and more. Corruption kills by letting gun makers push assault rifles into the hands of mentally unbalanced young males. And corruption kills pedestrians by giving preference to cars. And the companion of corruption is despair. Life is so unfair.

D: The companion of corruption is despair. If that's from the Bible I've never heard it, but I agree.

De: Get to the point.

D: Vote splittin's at the root of unfair elections, so do ya wanna hear about it?

De: Tell me how you think it makes a difference.

D: Here's a way to understand it. Suppose there's four politicians.

De: What are their names?

D: I dunno. This is just a generic example.

De: Generic is dead, like the people here. Let's bring them to life.

D: Ya can raise the dead?

De: Not those thems. You describe the politicians, I'll name them.

D: Two of them are puppets of wealthy business owners. The other two promise to reduce corruption, so they're more popular.

De: The names of the first two are Pinoke and Oscar.

D: Huh?

De: Pinoke is a nickname for Pinnochio, the puppet. And Oscar's last name is the Grouch. He lives in trash just like corrupt politicians.

D: OK. Whatever.

De: The other two are named Barack, 'cause we can hope for change, and Hypatia 'cause women should rule.

D: With these live candidates, two cruel, 'n two cool, who'd win?

De: Either Barack or Hypatia, the cool ones, obviously. 'Cause voters want less corruption. Voters don't want Pinoke, don't want Oscar. Don't want the cruel ones. They're the puppets of money-obsessed, short-sighted, large-mouthed, bass. The turds.

D: Right. The voters get what they crave. But then big-money donors arrive.

De: Now you'll say their money buys ads. Filled with lies. Ones that hypnotize. The way a car's headlights freeze a bumpkin rabbit.

D: Only the terminally stupid'd fall for those.

De: So tell me why you think money changes who prevails.

D: Wealthy business owners, who're the biggest campaign donors, in private they choose which puppet gets their dollars. If they like the look of Pinoke's nose, then Oscar's paid to hike the Appalachian trail.

De: That would make Oscar a grouch. What if he should refuse?

D: They point out he'll lose. Without their funds. Or they bribe him with favors, if that's what it takes.

De: A dumpster full of cookies and cakes?

D: Or a lifetime subscription to Grouch Magazine. That'd leave Pinoke and Hypatia and Barack. Who'd win that three-way race?

De: Ah, lots of voters would split their votes between Barack and Hypatia. That would make it easier for Pinoke to reach victory.

D: Plus Cock Brothers 'n Forbes levels of dollars'd pay for ads that attack Hypatia and Barack. Plus there'd be news coverage that favors Pinoke. That'd tip the three-way balance enough to rig the race.

De: A puppet wins. That's unfair! More voters prefer either Barack or Hypatia.

D: That's what I've learned. But now I'm stuck. I dunno how to change elections so they don't suck.

De: We can call attention to this deception. We'll get voters to concentrate their votes on either Barack or Hypatia.

D: But who decides which candidate to boycott?

De: We the voters could get one to vacate their spot, right?

D: That'd coax the wealthiest campaign donors to quietly fund yet another candidate. One who also promises to reduce corruption.

De: Let's name this one Ghandi. Yes, that tactic's difficult to beat. It splits the voters even more, making it even harder to defeat their single puppet.

D: All that happens in primary elections. Then in the general elections we have to choose between the two puppets, one from each party.

De: Now it makes even more sense why we have just two main political parties.

D: Yup. 'N years ago the puppet politicians passed a law to give funding to a third-party presidential candidate who gets more than five percent of the vote.

De: I thought that was a good thing. Now I see it's a bribe that seduces liberals into splitting their votes. While conservatives quietly concentrate their votes on just one candidate.

D: You learn fast.

De: I have a degree in political science. But they never taught us this.

D: The unfairness is hidden. It's hidden by numbers. 'N math to me is scary.

De: What scares me is that our planet is losing snow leopards and elephants, and rhinoceros ... es, and dolphins, and coral reefs, and plant species that could cure and feed.

D: Is that what got you into politics?

De: At the age of fourteen, ... my mother died. And that's when lots of people, especially my father, lied. They told me everything would turn out fine.

D: Can't imagine what it'd be like to lose someone so close. At such a young age.

De: I can't either. It's still unreal. Especially because just six years later my brother was killed in the Middle East.

D: How did ya cope?

De: I almost lost hope. Then I realized my mother was killed by incompetent city officials. They failed to time pedestrian walk signals to give walkers a head start. Before the green light for cars. And I realized, that basically, my brother was in the Middle East defending profits for oil companies.

D: Is that why we're in this graveyard?

De: My mother and brother are buried over there.

D: Ah.

De: Over time I traced the local corruption to the state level, then to DC, then to Congress where they write laws so corrupt.

D: Yeah, corruption. I wouldn't know how to define it, but I sure see lots of it.

De: It's betrayal, bribery, cheating, and coersion, cronyism, deceitfulness, deluding, and deviousness, dishonesty, duplicity, and embezzelment, entrapment, fraud, and graft, misleading, misrepresenting, and monopolizing, palm greasing, payoffs, profiteering, swindling, and tax evasion, treachery, underhandedness, unethicalness, unscrupulousness, and wickedness. Not to mention lying, and littering.

D: Littering?

De: It's a gateway crime.

D: Huh?

De: Like not picking up dog poop, 'n not wearing a face mask during an epidemic.

D: You're dancing to the beat of different bongos now Desha.

D: A dog owner doesn't pick up her dog's poop because her dog will poke the poop with his snout. She picks it up so other dogs don't poke the poop with their snouts. In return, those other dog owners protect her dog by picking up their dog's poop. It's a poop scooper loop. And the same kind of loop works with face masks during an epidemic, but with coughs and sneezes and spittle instead of poop.

D: 'Kay ....

De: In other words, corruption's a situation where someone gets something by not caring about the suffering of others.

D: That makes sense.

De: I have a tatoo that helps remind me to keep fighting that fight.

D: Against people who don't, er, participate in ... super pupper ... pooper scooper loops?

De: Fighting against corruption.

D: Can I see it? The tatoo?

De: It's for private tours only.

D: What's it say?

De: After I realized what really killed my mother and brother, I decided to kick some ... sense into the political world. The tatoo's a reminder to kick ass, and not stop fighting.

D: Who're you fighting?

De: I've hit another turning point. Originally I thought the enemy was capitalism, and that socialism was the opposite. But it's not that simple.

D: Hopefully that wasn't on the tatoo.

De: Then I fought against the Republican party. But recently I realized the Democratic party has been infiltrated by big-money donors.

D: I too just learned the Republican party isn't the real enemy.

De: My anger is getting thick. So I need to figure out, and soon, whose butt I should kick.

D: I think I've found the tipping point.

De: The place on a cow that can tip them over? Metaphorically.

D: Yeah, but that path's blocked.

De: By what?

D: Math. It's blockin' me from findin' the whackin' point that'll turn the world right side up.

De: I got as far as algebra and trig and statistics without using my protractor to commit suicide. What d'you wanna figure out?

D: How should votes be counted to get results that're fair?

De: I don't know. But I do know someone who might know the answer.

D: Can he teach us ...

De: Wrong gender.

D: Who's she?

De: She's a math prof.

D: A professor of math?

De: Yes, at the state university. That's where I got my degree.

D: I couldn't cope with even community college. Textbooks hold weasely words that try to drag my brain to useless destinations.

De: So you're a loser.

D: Huh?

De: You're giving up. Instead of fighting. That's losing.

D: I'm not brainy. So that'd be a bloodbath. How do I fight against math?

De: What took you this far?

D: I'm short 'n not handsome. So workin' hard to earn money is all I've got to keep my fiancé happy.

De: What's so special about her?

D: She appreciates me, even though I'm notta hunk surrounded by adoring women.

De: All you need is one.

D: Do you have someone?

De: I have my Willie.

D: Whoa! Say what?

De: My partner's name is Willie.

D: Are you two happy? You and your ... Willie?

De: What's your disability? Did you never learn to read? Do the words migrate across the page like musk oxen? Do you nod off on page seven and knock ... your hot cocoa on your ... lap? There's gotta be somethin' more.

D: It hurts to read. The words're a pain to look at.

De: Have you tried color-tinted glasses?

D: Huh? I don't follow.

De: Of course not. Never mind. I'll send you to a website where you can find out more. In the meantime, do you want me to arrange a meeting with this math prof?

D: 'Kay but only if she can explain things in spoken words 'n not use jargon, or academic speak.

De: I think she can be OK with that. 'N maybe I can translate if that's what you really need.

D: It's what I need. What d'you need?

De: In my early youth we were so happy then, even though money we lacked. Then the world turned upside down. I saw suffering that I hadn't seen before. And I traced the cause of suffering to corruption in government. Those ... grrr ... politicians are the puppets of greedy money huggers. Now I want to send them to hell, those greedy evil buggers.

D: Which ones? The politicians or the big campaign contributors?

De: Whoever is most to blame.

D: From what I can tell, they're both clueless. About why money works so well as puppet strings. Can we fight against cluelessness?

De: If it would help to carry a banner that says End Cluelessness Now I will. I'll fight against whoever's to blame.

D: So what's your plan?

De: I call on the spirits of the dead here to inspire me to find a way to shine a light on corruption. I want a light that's so bright that even evil money huggers and egotistic puppets cannot fight back. Not with their wealth, not with lawyers, and not with help from momma blogger Ramalama from Alabama. I know that light casts out darkness. In that light the path to better times will appear. Until that happens I'll keep on fighting. Fighting with words. Fighting with deeds. Doing whatever it takes to bring good times back to us, the voters, the majority.

D: Whatever it takes? Yeah, it seems it'll take some lookin' into mathematics.

De: I'm willing to help you fight against your fear of math. Let's do this!

D: I'm ready to fight! I just hope there's no evil fractions. Otherwise I'm ready to find out how to turn the world right side up.

De: Rise up dead spirits! Help us avenge your deaths at the hands of the corrupt!
