Scene 3, Behind The Political Curtain

D (Damien): [Speaks to himself.]  Hmm.  That's an odd campaign slogan.  ... Let's put corruption behind us.  ... Ah, maybe this is him.

F (Congressman Fred Finckhster): Thank you for waiting.

D: Thank you for your time Congressman Finckhster.

F: Come into this conference room.  And pardon the mess.  I keep hearing promises.  That someone will clean this all up.  Alas, it was hit by a fresh tomato.  I mean tornado.

D: I knew politics was messy.

F: While I'm looking for bugs, would you like some cold pizza?  The sausage is pork.

D: Not if that's where the bugs have been holding their rallies.  And the cheese looks more counterfeit than a spray-on tan.

F: Oh man, not those kinds of bugs.  We politicians need to keep our secrets unknown.  From political spies.  In fact, turn off your phone, and I'll tell you no lies.

D: Deal!  Powering down.  No live feed.  No recording.

F: Jordan told me to trust you with behind-the-curtain and under-the-trap-door kinds of magic we use on the political stage.

D: What a treat!  A wise sage!

F: Of course you realize we have to tell lies.  To voters.  If we told the dumb voters what we tell our rich donors, we would never win our prize, re-election.

D: I suspect your donors don't forget your promises.

F: Indeed they don't.  They hire lobbyists to keep track of us.  And they make such a fuss if we just vote for a bill that might drop their gross income by the price of one chocolate mousse.

D: That is gross.

F: Yet the biggest political trick isn't deceit.  It's distraction.

D: You've got my full attention.

F: Name calling is part of the distraction.  Some Democrats refer to some Republicans as greedy capitalists, one percenters, climate deniers, misogynists, racists, Nazis, dimwits, and liars.  Some Republicans refer to some Democrats as socialists, communists, tree huggers, immigrants, abortionists, feminists, Nazis, dimwits, and liars.  Those exaggerated right-and-left names entertainingly distract from the fact there's a bigger conflict.

D: What's bigger than the fight between left and right?

F: Allow me to introduce you to a new way to view the political terrain.

D: My brain is open wide and ready to be stupefied.

F: The much bigger fight is between the political up and the political down.

D: Political up?  Political down?  Where did left and right go to?  What's up?

F: The political up, that's where most voters are.

D: Voters of which party?

F: Both parties.

D: How can that be?  Both Republicans and Democrats are in the political up?

F: Stop thinking of voters as Republicans and Democrats.  It's us politicians, and the party insiders, who are Republicans and Democrats.

D: So is this like Star Wars fans also can be Star Trek fans?  And the opposite?

F: Heck, if this is news to you, maybe you too have failed to recognize that both parties are controlled by people who utilize money.

D: That part I knew.

F: The biggest campaign contributors are in the political down.

D: The biggest contributors?  Should we be putting down fat people?

F: Not that kind of biggest.  They give the biggest amounts of money.  They provide the money source that lifts us politicians high in the polls.  They have bought our souls.  Without their dough we get dashed against the rocks during a primary.

D: You're talking about rich folks, right?

F: Of course they are all rich.  But some of the rich are in the political up.

D: How can that be?  I'm as confused as if I just stepped out from an industrial laundry washer during the high-spin cycle.

F: Confused by the spin.  That's the state most voters are in.  That's the aim of the political down.  So of course they resist adopting a name.

D: But among themselves they call themselves the political down?

F: No.  That's just how I think of them.

D: This upfront lowdown is blindingly illuminating.

F: Of course lots of wealthy folks in the political down prefer our party, the Republican party.

D: Even I can see that.

F: Yet many people in the political down have infiltrated the Democratic party.

D: Voting in both parties?  That's illegal.

F: You're thinking of votes.  I'm talking about money.

D: Whoa!  How come the political down gives money to both left and right parties?  That's as stupid as placing bets on both horses in a two-horse race.

F: Ah, here's a big secret.  They assist horses, er, candidates, in their disliked party only during primary elections.  Of course I'm dumbing things down.

D: That fits my brain.  Now I see!  They use money to influence who wins each Democratic primary!  But in the general election they just embrace the puppet candidate they like better?

F: Isn't it brilliant?  Yet seductive.  Like the gleaming smile of a fetching lass in an auto ad.

D: Now I'm beginning to see how come we, er, most voters, dislike both candidates, the Republican and the Democrat.  I don't mean any disrespect.

F: A lack of respect is what we politicians expect.  We know we're stuck in the middle.  Between voters and our biggest campaign contributors.  Now do you see why voters are distracted by the entertaining conflict between left and right?

D: Instead of words I need a picture.

F: It's sort of like when ancient Roman rulers distracted their plebeians with dramatic bloody fighting in their colossal Colosseum.

D: So it's like funding two cults, or two gangs, that fight each other on stage?  While the funders quietly remove money from our wallets while we're distracted?

F: That's a bit rough.  But close enough.

D: The fighters who attacked Congress sure were duped by the fake fight between left and right.  They believed their cult was defending democracy.

F: Indeed, their vision was out of focus.

D: With their clout, what does the political down really care about?

F: Profits for the businesses they own.  What especially delights them are business tax breaks.  And when they can get a legal monopoly they grasp onto that like a Titanic passenger would cling to a spare life vest.

D: Wow!  Those business owners pay politicians for lower taxes and no competition.  That deal's sweet!  But that's cheating.  It's corrupt.  Am I wrong?

F: You're right.

D: I'm confused.  On the political right, the Republican party seems to care about some non-business issues like abortions, and gay marriage, Christian values, immigration, and such, right?

F: They do glorify those things on the flag they fly.  But taxes, and subsidies, and monopolies are their real priorities.  That's because party insiders, in both parties, get their marching orders from the biggest campaign contributors.

D: Now I think I'm seeing how come neither political party really gives a damn about giving homeless folks an assist, ending school shootings, helping Latinos who don't vote.  Or listening to what dolphins have to say about us dumping trash into their swimming pool.  I see the down folks are using religious and racial and gender issues to divide and distract.

F: And remember it's the biggest contributors.  Some kinds of small businesses get overlooked.  Unless they join with similar businesses and hire lobbyists to look out for their shared interests.

D: Ah, you have to ignore any businesses that can't afford lobbyists.

F: We know we can't please everyone.  ...  Here's another diversion tactic.  Money from the right can fund extreme agendas on the left.  Like when the LGB category got extended to include queers and transgenders.  That scares lots of voters to the right.

D: Yeah, many voters blindly believe the enemy of their enemy is their friend.

F: That's a powerful political tactic.

D: It's foolish.  Because no one can keep all their enemies in the opposite team.  Not with just two teams.

F: Here's something else you should know.  It's easy to hide the giving of funds to both sides, left and right.  In fact there are two brothers in Florida in the sugar biz.  One brother is a Republican who gives buckets of money to Republican politicians.  The other brother is a Democrat who gives buckets of money to Democratic politicians.  Isn't that sweet?

D: And as hard to follow as a rat chased by a cat.  So they don't give a damn about left or right?  They just lobby for sugar ...

F: Subsidies.  Especially tax money that pays for canals in Florida so farmers can grow sugarcane.  They need that for the bigger financial gains that come from converting sugarcane into sugar.  That's what's done by the businesses those bros own.

D: That's a sour deal for us.  How come this isn't more obvious?

F: On commodity price lists, they used to list sugar and U.S. sugar by those names.  But now the names are sugar number eleven and sugar number sixteen.

D: That's a slick trick.  Um, there's something else that's a bit fuzzy.  Don't some rich folks prefer the Democratic party?

F: Lots of lawyers get lots of their business from Democratic voters, so they like Democratic politicians.  And then there are industries that subdivide into left and right.

D: Huh?  I thought I was following you down main street, but you just ducked into a dark alley.

F: Banks prefer the Republican party.  And credit unions prefer the Democratic party.

D: Wow!  I think now I'm seeing who's in the political down.  And how come they oppose us, the voters, their suckers, in the political up.  But how do so very few rich folks outvote us, the non-rich, who outnumber them?  Like ants outnumber humans doing cleanup at picnics.

F: There are lots of tricks to control politics.  For example, do you know what delegates are?

D: Sure, that's the laundry cycle women use to wash their underwear.

F: You're thinking of delicates with a Cee.  This is delegates with a Gee.  A delegate is someone you vote for and they vote for the actual candidate.

D: Like the electoral college?

F: Sort of like that.  But here I'm talking about a primary election.  The one before the nomination convention.

D: The conventions I know about sell computer games, comic books, and whacky kitchen gadgets.

F: A political convention doesn't sell anything.  At least not directly.  During a primary election, when you vote for a governor, secretary of state, or president, you're actually voting for someone who has promised to vote for the candidate whose name you mark on your ballot.  Those people, the ones who get enough votes, are delegates.  They travel to a convention where they vote on your behalf.

D: Why isn't my vote counted directly?

F: Sometimes, none of the candidates gets a majority of votes.  There has to be enough support to justify having just one nominee on the general election ballot.  So at the convention the delegates do rounds of voting until one candidate gets a majority.  That's half the votes.

D: So we're at a nomination convention, where one of the delegates is voting on my behalf.

F: After each round of voting, if there's no majority, the candidate with the fewest delegate votes usually drops out.  Traditionally that candidate tells their delegates who to vote for in the next round.

D: Now I'm the one spinning round and round in the laundry machine.

F: It can get very messy.  There used to be lots of backroom deals involved.  Like promises of political appointments in return for a candidate telling their delegates to vote for the more popular candidate who has made that promise.

D: Oh, I see.  Lots of tricks go on to influence the delegates.

F: Yes.  Sometimes literally.  In fact sexual bribery was common before women got the right to vote.

D: Whoa!  Trading a vote for sex?  That's a kind of bribery, ... I didn't know about.

F: Surely you know, wealthy people pay lots of different kinds of helpers to influence everyone who's involved in politics.

D: I hadn't known about bribery using prostitutes.  I suppose this category includes hiring social-media influencers, celebrities, and lobbyists?

F: Yes.  Here's another twist.  At the Democratic national election, where a presidential candidate is chosen, there are super delegates who weren't elected by the voters.

D: Wow, super extra layers of corrup..., of extra influence.  Just to get this majority thingy.  Why is that so damned important?

F: There's something in politics called vote splitting.

D: What do spitting goats have to do with politics?

F: Not goats.  Votes.  As in voting.

D: Vote spitting?

F: Not spitting, although spitting used to happen in Congress so often they had spittoons.  It's splitting, vote splitting.  It's the election version of divide and conquer.

D: Oh, vote splitting.

F: That's it!

D: And divide and conquer.  Isn't that the battle tactic armies use when they separate enemy soldiers from one another?  And then they can attack the smaller groups when they are aloner?

F: Yes.  In fact, voting is rooted in war and fighting.  That's why women weren't allowed to vote.  They weren't counted because they don't fight.

D: Those rule makers never met my uncle's ex-wife.

F: So voting started out as counting to figure out how many men would be in each army.

D: And two small armies would lose against one bigger army.

F: Yes.  If the big army can fight just one small army at a time.  That's what dividing the enemy enables.  That's why Lincoln said a house divided cannot stand.

D: Oh, so long ago voting began as a way to count potential army sizes.  And the smaller army surrenders?

F: Saves a lot of dying, no?  And when splitting can't be done physically, then splitting psychologically works too.  In politics, that's what religious and racial and gender differences do.

D: So how does this vote splitting work?

F: Well, it involves math.

D: Oh dear, you mean, mathematics?

F: Certainly.

D: In my opinion math is the devil's favorite form of torture.  If I had to choose between doing just one page of math homework, or standing under a tall fir tree getting cut down, I would be there yelling timber!

F: I don't like it either.  And I don't understand vote splitting well enough to clearly explain how it all works.  I just know that it works.  So those of us in Congress, when we cast our votes, are hobbled and handcuffed and gagged.

D: And your noses must be stuffed up to not notice the stink.  So now I think I understand distractions.  Yet how can they so easily hide this vote splitting?

F: You've heard of the electoral college?  And gerrymandering?

D: I admit long ago I wondered if Jerry Mander was someone important.  Now I realize those are about the attention-grabbing fight between Republicans and Democrats.

F: That's why news reporters are not blocked from writing and speaking about the electoral college and gerrymandering.

D: Which now I know helps distract from, and hide, the bigger fight between political up and political down.

F: And you've heard of the money bonus that a third-party presidential candidate can get if they get more than a certain percent of the vote?

D: Aha!  That's a tactic to split votes away from ... well who?

F: In the U.S., third parties are mostly on the left.

D: Why is that?

F: The political right has more money.  So the leader of a small conservative party gets a bribe to stand aside.

D: Those leaders fall for that?

F: Wouldn't you if they offered you a political job with a good salary?  Or a tricked-out RV?  Or a vacation in Tahiti?

D: I admit the tricked-out RV parked in Tahiti would do it.

F: Also the wealthy right quietly and indirectly donates money to boost third parties on the left.  That splits votes away from the Democratic nominee.  Which concentrates conservative voters' ballots on the single Republican nominee.

D: And now I see how come news media and those slippery political ads don't have as much influence as many folks suppose.  The splitting of votes among candidates during primaries, that's their key to controlling you politicians.  Like puppets.  Er, well, like I'm not sure what.

F: That's not what I like to hear.  Yet between you and me, the political down is my puppet master, the puppeteer.

D: And yet we have social media.  Aren't there some bright wits calling attention to these dirty politics?

F: To deal with that the political down hires critics who write biased comments in forums.  Like claiming evidence that a candidate keeps underage sex slaves in his basement.  Or a fake photo of an elderly candidate falling asleep while walking down stairs.

D: Conspiracies!

F: And they hire coders to make bots that upvote their propaganda, and downvote folks who try to reveal their secrets.

D: Can't voters see through that fake smoke and mirrors?

F: The political down carefully tracks and targets voters who are gullible.  Such as the ones who believe that eggs, colorful ones no less, come from rabbits.

D: On Easter.  Yes, I've never understood that either.  Now I know how come some people online attack with such anger.  Lots of them are getting paid to be a faker?

F: Yup.  Mister Mischievous from Michigan is getting paid to inspire Furious Fiona from Florida to re-post the clever meme he created that trashes Robert Reformer who supports removing assault weapons from the hands of bullied Billy Barbarian who spends his nights playing Gun Survival Fantasy.  As you know, attack ads, the political kind, really work.  Lots of voters get swayed by believing that expression the enemy of my enemy is my friend.

D: That's stupid.  Don't they see that just means they are shooting at the same target?

F: And getting hit by friendly fire!  Yes that particular stupidity is especially easy to exploit.  And more common than blues venues in Detroit.

D: I think I'm figuring this out.  It's not what I expected.  Yet it makes too much sense to be fictional.

F: There's one bit of history I should teach you.

D: Oh I hate history.  Cramming more facts about the Alamo and the Civil War into my head might crowd out what I've stuffed in here.

F: This is simple.  Back when primary elections were uncommon, vote splitting was obvious.  There was just one election.  The one we now call the general election.  Sometimes a political party offered two or even three candidates.  If the other big party offered just one candidate, that solo one won.

D: Oh, because voters in the losing party would split their votes among their similar candidates.  The divide and conquer tactic.

F: The winning strategy came to be for each party to have their own primary.  The idea was to discover which politician deserves that party's solo spot on the general election ballot.

D: Ah!  Now the splitting is hidden in primaries.  Where the winner is never from the wrong party.  And the winner is always a puppet.

F: Indeed!  You learn fast!  So here's a test.

D: Oh dear lord!  I hate tests!  And this might be about history no less.

F: Let go of that foolish fear.  This answer should be clear.

D: [Sighs.] Okay.

F: What helped Barack Obama win the two thousand eight Democratic primary election against Hillary Clinton?

D: Based on what you've taught, some wealthy business owners may have thought, ... that well, ...

F: You're on the right track.

D: ... thought to give money to Obama to defeat their bigger enemy, Clinton.

F: Correct so far.

D: And they probably made the assumption that a black man couldn't win the general election.  Is that right?

F: You did it!  That's right!  Although of course we cannot trace all donations.  Yet when we can track it, that donation pattern often happens.

D: The Republicans ... er, fans of Republican politicians, who gave contributions to Obama must have chuckled when they donated.  Then raged for days when they got an African American president.

F: And that gives you an example of a blocking tactic that wouldn't work if vote splitting were not such a useful gimmick for rigging elections.

D: I heard what you said.  But the words leaked out of my brain without leaving behind any idea about what they mean.

F: If unfair vote splitting were to disappear, then that blocking kind of tactic wouldn't interfere.  That's because in the general election, each party would offer two or three selections, candidates.

D: Now I'm confused again.  How can there be two Republicans and two Democrats on the ballot?  That would cause vote splitting.  Some things are becoming clear.  But what can make the evil split disappear?

F: I really don't know, and don't care.  The political stress was making a mess of my ulcers.  Giving them their own ulcers.  So I won't be running for re-election.  That's why I can risk speaking candidly.  But remember you didn't learn any of this from me.  If you go to newspaper reporters with these insights I'll shine light on the skeletons in the closet of your friend Jordan.  He asked me to help you on your mission to earn money using his tricks.

D: I won't be a snitch or fink.  And anyways I can't yet link the problem of vote splitting to a solution.  One that can defeat this unfairness.  So I need to learn more.  Do you know who would know how to fix this mess?  The unfair politics?

F: Probably that would be a professor of mathematics.

D: Aiee!  Scholastic gymnastics!  In math no less.  That's too advanced.  I'm almost peeing in my pants, it scares me so!

F: Well I've got to go.  I wish you luck in understanding yet more so maybe someday you can help clean up the muck on the political stage.  In the meantime, the political world is upside down.  Voters mistakenly assume the rich are at the top.  Yes, politicians have all been bought.  But as voters you've got more power than the Space X rockets, a swarm of locusts, and a flu virus, all working together on the same team.

D: So you think maybe someday the voters will, uh, ...

F: ... reign supreme?  Eventually.  Hopefully.  Then us politicians will become your puppets.  But currently we're the puppets of wealthy business owners.

D: I admit I'm seeing a bit of hope for a fix.  To get less dysfunctional politics.  Now I know the greedy rich distract us with left versus right.  Yet up versus down, that's the real fight.

F: You've got it right.  Good luck!  But don't forget, it was some other schmuck who gave you the tour of the elephant and donkey muck.

D: Thank you for tilting my brain to better see the political terrain.  You've saved me from going insane.
