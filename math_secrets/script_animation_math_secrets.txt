1: Algebra is the solving of mysteries

Image: young Albert Einstein scratching head in classroom, with other kids in background

The great mathematician Albert Einstein, when he was a child, struggled when he was first learning algebra.  Fortunately a wise uncle advised Albert to think of algebra as a mystery where the purpose is to become a detective and discover the hidden identity of a Mister X.

Image: words: mystery, secret, hidden, clue, puzzle

Imagine the fun children could have if algebra teachers presented mysteries and puzzles with clues, secret knowledge, and hidden insights.

The basics of algebra, starting with addition and subtraction, should be taught at an early age. Specifically, kids should be taught that adding and subtracting the same number to or from both sides of an equation is a useful way to calculate an unknown quantity.  A year or two later they can learn the trick of multiplying and dividing both sides of an equation by the same number.

During this early learning, advanced topics such as polynomials, the order of operations, and when to use capital or lowercase letters as unknown values, must be ignored.

When students reach high school they will not have to be taught the basics for solving an equation to discover an unknown quantity.  Instead they can focus on learning the traditional notation and terminoloy conventions that will be tested on college-entrance exams.  That notation will include the exponential notation, the order of operations, polynomials, logarithms, and other topics most of them will never need after college.

This recommended path will introduce them to negative numbers early.  Those are easy to teach by first limiting math exercises to the numbers negative one, negative two, negative three, negative four, and negative five.  They can encounter these simple negative numbers on a printed-on-paper ruler that extends from negative five on the left to positive five on the right, with zero in the middle.  They will learn that adding positive integers amounts to counting to the right, subtracting positive integers amounts to counting to the left, and the counting can temporarily involve the negative integers to the left of zero.  One year later they will be ready to learn the math shortcut of adding and subtracting negative numbers.

In hindsight it will become clear that what teachers have been referring to as math "problems" are 
really exercises, tasks, and puzzles.

Real-life problems, the mystery-solving kind, require practice learning to look at clues, discover patterns, and struggle to gain insights.  Very importantly, students need to become comfortable solving real-life problems, which cannot be solved by following rigorous step-by-step processes.

Throughout the last couple of centuries countless students have correctly guessed they were being taught excessively rigorous details that aren't especially valuable for solving the real-life math problems they will encounter as an adult.  A big clue about this fact comes from the fact that lots of parents can't help their children with math homework.

The skill of learning how to solve a mystery has always been valuable.  It's the basis for solving real-life problems.  This skill has become even more important now that artificial intelligence can ace any college-entrance math exam.

2: Division is also for comparing, not just splitting and scaling

Do you get caught in the trap of thinking about division as if it's mostly about splitting a whole pizza into equal-size slices?  And dealing with fractions?

Image: pizza slices, fractions

If so, your math teachers failed to explain that division is a tool that has many different uses.

As an analogy, the excessive focus on division as a tool for splitting is like giving someone a multi-tool or swiss army knife and only showing them how to use the knife blade.  The many other fold-out tools within the multi-tool are also available, but ignored, and unused.

So here's the next big secret.  When you get stumped by a real-life math problem, solving the math probably involves division.

Why division?  If the math just involves addition and subtraction, the solution is obvious.  This is basically just accounting.

If the math involves multiplication, it's easy to recognize it as a shortcut for counting using big steps.  Such as counting by fives while you count on your fingers to seven, to reach the number thirty five.  That's easy, so you know when multiplication is needed.

But division is different.  To use the mystery metaphor, division has many disguises and aliases.

In particular, here are some words that refer to special kinds of division: ratio, proportion, rate, slope, reciprocal, percent, and the word "per."

Schools delay teaching these deeper uses of division as if high school is when student are ready to learn these math concepts.

Even worse, the failure to fully teach the "why" behind division makes later academic subjects harder to understand.  This is why so many students struggle to understand chemistry, physics, trigonometry, and  statistics.  A deep understanding of division is needed for these academic subjects.

Here's a quick way to gain a deeper understanding of division.  It's often used to compare things.

If one person travels five miles in twenty minutes, and another person travels seven miles in fifteen minutes, who traveled faster?  Division makes it easy to calculate the average speed in miles per minute.  The first person traveled zero point two five miles per minute, and the second person traveled zero point four seven miles per minute.

To avoid keeping track of how many zeros are involved, we change the units from minutes to hours.  To do that we multiply by sixty because there are sixty minutes in an hour.  That gives us fifteen miles per hour and twenty eight miles per hour.  This result makes it clear the second person was traveling much faster.

How much faster?  The best way to compare these two speeds is is to divide one by the other.  This gives us the ratio of twenty eight compared to fifteen, which equals one point eight seven.  Converting this number to a percent comparison tells us the second person travelled eighty seven percent faster than the first person.

Here's another tip.  If you aren't sure whether the bigger or smaller number goes on the top of the fraction, estimate whether the result should be bigger than one or smaller than one.  If bigger, the bigger number goes on top.  If smaller, the smaller number goes on top.

Using the same two sample numbers, dividing fifteen by twenty eight gives us zero point five three four.  This means the slower person travelled at a speed that was fifty three percent of the faster person.

Of course this level of precision isn't useful in this simple speed example.  However, in real-life professions your calculations may involve the possibility of a bridge collapsing, or a patient dying, or ....

When students are taught to do a deeper dive into what division really does, including all of its many uses, real-life math problems become much easier to solve.

Another math betrayal about division is to only teach young students the division symbol that has dots above and below the horizontal line.  When those students grow up and use computers and spreadsheet apps they discover computer keyboards don't not have that key, and spreadsheet apps expect the slash symbol to represent division.

Here's yet another betrayal.  Math classes frequently represent division where the top number (the "numerator") and the bottom number (the "denominator") are separated by a long horizontal line.  In real life, using computers and spreadsheet apps, students must learn the left-to-right orientation, where the fraction's top number is entered first, the slash symbol is entered next, and then the bottom number is entered.

The big pattern here is that attempting to excessively simplify division leads to math confusion years later when a full understanding of division will make lots of college-level subjects much easier.

3: Fractions are for communication, not calculation
Images: ancient Romans

Centuries ago ancient people didn't know how to divide three by four.  So they left the fraction of three fourths as a fraction.  They wrote down the fraction as if it was a final number.

When they needed to add and subtract those fractions they developed clever tricks, such as dividing the top and bottom numbers by two, and repeating that trick as long as both numbers are even numbers.  And they looked for what we now refer to as the lowest common denominator.  Sometimes those simplifications made it much easier to add and subtract the fractions and to get a useful simpler number.  That's clever.

Briefly teaching those clever fraction tricks at a young age is a useful history lesson.  It helps children better appreciate how we do math today.

However, we now have the decimal number system.  It easily allows us to divide three by four.  The result is zero point seven five.

Are fractions obsolete?  Not at all!

When someone asks you to fill their glass "two thirds full," that's much clearer than asking you to fill it "sixty six percent full."

Fractions are very useful for communication.

However, fractions are not useful when doing calculations.  In that case, a fraction is a division task that has not yet been done.

When students learn to do math using a spreadsheet app, they discover fractions cannot be typed into a spreadsheet cell.  And they never see a fraction in any calculated result.

Cursive writing has been removed from school lessons because it's obsolete.  Now it's time to remove the obsolete skill of looking for common denominators in numbers expressed as fractions.

The concepts behind some of the clever tricks for simplifying fractions certainly needs to be taught.  They are similar to tricks used in algebra when the numbers are unknown.

However, adding and subtracting fractions expressed in numbers is not useful in the modern world.

So when you see or hear a fraction with specific numbers, recognize it's a useful way to communicate a quantity.  However, one of the first steps in any any calcuation that involves a numeric fraction is to do the indicated division.  Then you can easily combine the result with other numbers.

4: Decimal inches are superior to fractional inches

Possibly the biggest loss of joy in math education happens when young students learn to use a ruler marked in quarters, eighths, and possibly sixteenths of an inch.

Splitting an inch into quarters and eighths has been obsolete for at least a few centuries.  Yet students in the United States are required to buy rulers with these obsolete markings.

Long ago, Europeans wisely switched to measuring distances using meters, centimeters, and millimeters.  These measurements are easy to add and subtract, in the same way dollars and cents are easy to add and subtract.

However, we don't need to switch to the metric system to gain the advantage of using decimal numbers.

Schools should be asking students to buy a ruler that shows decimal inches.

Also those rulers should go up to ten inches, not twelve inches.  Those ten inches provide a useful way to visualize percentages.  That's because each tic mark is one percent of that ruler's total marked length.

Why do schools waste time teaching students how to read fractions from a ruler as if inches can only be measured using fractions?

Tradition!  That's the way it was done for centuries.

And it provides an excuse for teaching how to add and subtract fractions, which you now know is also foolish.

Some adult professions still require employees to use fractional inches.  However, that tradition will continue to fade away.  Already workers in the construction industry can buy a "decimal tape measure" which is sometimes called an "engineering" tape measure.  Alas, cloth tape measures for sewing are not yet easy to find.

If you ever find yourself trying to add or subtract fractional inches, please consider buying a ruler, or metal tape measure, or cloth tape measure marked in decimal inches.

If you should need to quickly convert between fractional and decimal inches without grabbing a calculator, choose to use a measuring device that shows both fractional and decimal inches on opposite sides of the same ruler or tape measure.

In the same way fractions are useful for communication, fractional inches are sometimes useful for communication.  However, in the same way fractions are not useful for calculations, fractional inches are not useful for calculations, not even addition and subtraction.

Schools should stop taking the joy out of math by insisting that part of an inch must be expressed as a fraction of an inch.

5: Units, pick one in each category when calculating

Europeans use the metric system, which measures time in seconds.  Yet two European friends don't say "Bye, see you in six hundred and four point eight kiloseconds" instead of saying "Bye, see you in a week."  Everyone, worldwide, speaks using the same familiar time units we use in the United States, namely minutes, hours, days, weeks, months, and years.

Some people think we should adopt metric units in the kitchen for food prep.  Yet that misses the point.  Recipes can continue to specify quantities in tablespoons, teaspoons, and cups.  And we can continue to conveniently specify other food quantities in pints, quarts, and liters.  And we can continue to use yards for football fields, and feet for cutting lumber, and inches for smaller sizes.

Only when we need to do calculations do we need to choose just one unit for each measurement category.

This concept is similar to the distinction between fractions and decimal numbers.  Fractions are fine for communication, but decimals are needed for calculations.  In a similar way, any unit can be used for communication, but choosing just one unit per category is necessary when doing calculations.

What are the unit categories?  Distance, time, and weight are the main categories.  Areas could be measured using squared distances such as square feet.  Volumes could be measured using cubic distances such as cubic inches.  Yet even the metric system measures volume using liters instead of using cubic meters.  So the category of volume in the kitchen is where we use teaspoons, cups, pints, and so on.

Although scientists promote the idea of switching to the metric system, they overlook the fact that seconds are not useful for specifying a day or a week or a year.

Some people will promote the idea of using the metric system for kitchen measurements of volume.  Ironically Europeans measure food quantities using a weight scale, which is at least party because cubic millimeters and cubic centimeters have their own complications.  Specifically, although ten millimeters equals one centimeter, one thousand cubic millimeters equals one cubic centimeter.

The complication with volume becomes clear when a baker mixes one teaspoon of salt with two cups of flour.  That's a ratio of ....  Cheap plastic measuring scoops easily handle this dramatic range of volumes.  Doing the equivalent using a digital weight scale requires an expensive precise electronic scale.

Different math situations require different units.  When a reporter asks how long it will take a spacecraft to reach the moon, the scientific answer expressed in seconds isn't useful.  Instead it needs to be communicated in days, not seconds.

When communicating, pick a unit that fits the situation.  When calculating, convert each number into the same specific unit in each category.  Then, if needed, convert the calculated result back into a meaningful unit for communication.

6: Triangles deserve deep understanding

Surveying is about angles, and angles are the basis of geometry and trigonometry.  Yet geometry and trigonometry courses don't include the foundational topic of surveying.

Even before children learn how to add and subtract they can draw three lines on a sheet of paper.  And they can be taught to draw those lines along a straightedge, which can be a piece of wood or plastic that has no numbers or markings.  And they can be taught to line up the straightedge with a distant location such as a tree outdoors or the corner of a room.

Surveying requires only one more simple concept.  One of the three lines needs to point to a table where the paper will be moved to.  At that second table the paper needs to be rotated so the line from the other table now points back to that table.  When the student draws a straight line to the same distant tree or classroom corner, that forms a triangle.  That triangle has the same angles, and the same ratio of distances, as the bigger equivalent triangle that connects the three physical locations.

Just by drawing more triangles to more locations young students can create a map.  Where the lines cross are the locations of those objects.  A playground can be mapped to show the locations of trees, fenceposts, and corners of buildings.  A map of a classroom would show where the walls meet at the corners.  Drawing straight lines to show fences, walls, and buildings completes the unlabelled map.  All without using any numbers.

Students will be drawing more precise maps by the time they learn how to multiply, divide, and measure decimal inches.  Then they will experience joy when they discover they can measure distant distances such as the width of a river cannot cross, the length of a fence without walking along it, and the lengths of buildings.  Those experiences will reveal the power of multiplication and division, and only measuring the distance between the two tables.

Measuring the height of a tree also uses a triangle.  It's just vertical instead of horizontal.

Here the math secret is that a deep understanding of triangles at an early age will make it much easier to understand advanced math topics.

When students learn about graphing linear functions they will discover the edges of the paper have been playing the role of the X and Y axes.  This means a specific point on the paper has an X value equal to the the distance to the paper's left edge, and has a Y value equal to the distance to the paper's bottom edge.

By the time students reach geometry class they will already understand foundational concepts such as identifying equal angles, parallel lines, and the importance of right angles.  Geometry teachers can more quickly teach college-entrance geometry topics such as the cleverest steps in Euclid's proofs, and the stereotypic two-column layout of math proofs.

Before students reach trigonometry class they will already know about the functions named sine, cosine, tangent, arcsine, arccosine, and arctangent.  Those are simply ratios among the sides of a right triangle.

Physics courses rely on students already having a deep understanding of right triangles, especially the ratio between horizontal distances and vertical distances.

Let's teach foundational concepts about triangles during much earlier years in school.  Let's not delay the subject until those concepts are needed in advanced courses such as geometry, trigonometry, and physics.

7: History of math enhances understanding math

Why do math teachers waste so much time teaching how to add and subtract fractions?

Which math event led to the Italian Renessance?

Why were logarithms especially useful for a couple of centuries?

Why is the "order of operations" not needed by non-mathemeticians?

Here are quick partial answers to these questions.  When students learn these parts of history they will better appreciate that earlier civilizations struggled with math for reasons that no longer apply to those of us now living.

Why do math teachers waste so much time teaching how to add and subtract fractions?  Ancient Romans used Roman numerals, which were designed for addition and subtraction and simple multiplication, but not division.  So ancient Romans didn't know what to do with a calculation such as three divided by four.  So they used the fraction as if it was a useful number.  When they added fractions they sometimes got a non-fractional result, so they developed clever tricks to add and subtract fractions.  This is where the trick of finding the "lowest common denominator" comes from.  Those math tricks eventually spread to Roman-ruled England, France, and Spain, and then to America.  Knowing about this history helps us appreciate why schools don't simply teach students to convert the fraction three fourths into a single number, namely zero point seven five.

Which math event led to the Italian Renessance?  The tipping-point event happened when a new Vatican pope demonstrated he could multiply and divide much faster than Romans who still used Roman numerals.  Until then the Vatican had declared the use of Arabic numerals to be a sin.  Those are the digits we now recognize as the digits zero through nine.  For clarification, the word "Arabic" became associated with these digits because the Koran is written in Arabic and that math concept passed through Islamic culture, having come from what is now India, where digits were used to represent bead patterns on the Chinese abacus.  Faster calcuations made it practical to adopt banking systems that employed accountants in different Italian cities.  Merchants using that banking system didn't need to carry precious cargo for trading, which put lots of highway robbers out of business, which increased travel between European cities.  Remember the artist genius Leanardo da Vinci was hired by the Medici family who gained immense wealth by owning a large banking system.

Why were logarithms especially useful for a couple of centuries?  Even using decimal digits the task of division is time-consuming.  One shortcut is to convert both the numbers to their logarithm equivalent, add those two logarithms, then convert back to the numbers they represent.  Originally these conversions were done using printed tables of numbers.  Keeping track of where the decimal place goes is done manually, without the tables.  Later this process was simplified by inventing the slide rule.  The sliding part is aligned so the two numbers line up under the cursor line, and the left end of the sliding part points to the resulting number.  These logarithmic methods competed against expensive hand-cranked and electric-motorized mechanical calculators until the first electronic calculators became available.

Why do math professors waste so much time teaching the "order of operations"? ... Long ago, math was done using sand or dust on a table as the writing surface.  Later it was done on paper using quill pens, which frequently had to have ink added from an inkwell.  Limiting the use of paretheses was helpful, both for conserving table space and ink, and for conserving the time it took to write parenteses, or their equivalent.  Now, typing extra parentheses on a keyboard is not a concern.  The increasing number of nested paretheses is not much of a concern, especailly because many text editors show which parenthesis matches which other parenthesis.  Yet we still minimize the number of paretheses as if we were still writing using quill pens, or doing calculations on sand tables.

Students deserve to learn about math history.  It's fascinating.  And it reveals why math follows traditions that have become obsolete centuries ago.

Speaking of which, lots of algebra is based on the book that first described the topic, which is named ....

Perhaps one reason math history isn't explicity taught is it would reveal our current math education traditions to be embarrassingly obsolete.

Expressed another way, it's like the theme in the movie and play named "Fiddler on the Roof."  In that story a younger generation asks "Why?" and the male head of the family answers with "Tradition!"

8: Terminology is much less important than concepts

When scientists work on solving a math challenge, rarely do they think in words such as "here's a variable" and "here's the unknown quantity."  When engineers design a bridge that uses triangles for stability, they don't name the trianges as "isocoles" or "right triangle."  And only a few people remember the name "Pythagorean theorem" when they remember that, for a right triangle, the sum of the squares of the two shorter sides equals the square of the longer side.

So why do math textbooks and standardized tests put so much emphasis on terminology?

A few decades ago, before videos and audio recordings became inexpensive to produce and play back, knowledge was written in books.  An easy way to find out if a student had read a chapter in a textbook was to ask questions about the definitions of terms defined in the chapter.

For this reason, textbook authors who were experts in their academic fields, were told to include terminology that they, the experts, did not themselves use in their own academic field.

Alas, public education, including math education, now waste excessive amounts of textbook space and classroom time teaching terminology with carefully worded definitions that won't be useful in the real worlds of business and government.

Instead it's the concepts behind those words that are important to learn.

Earlier you learned that division is hidden in words such as ratio, proportion, rate, and reciprocals.  Yet those words are not important for solving real challenges as long as the underlying concepts are clearly understood.

Of course terminology becomes important when communicating through words.  However, the internet, Wikipedia, and AI services can quickly supply the correct term, or the definition of a term, when those terms and definitions are needed.

Math education should reduce the number of math terms being taught in school.  Let's update video and audio learning resources so they don't make the same mistake as textbooks, namely an excessive focus on terminology and not enough focus on clearly understanding the underlying concepts.

9:Testing math distracts from teaching math

Multiple choice tests are obsolete.  Yet high schools spend lots of time teaching students how to do well on multiple choice tests.  Wealthy parents spend lots of money on experts who teach students how to get higher scores on multiple choice tests.  And universities heavily rely on multiple-choice test scores when deciding who to admit.

Computers, smart phones, well-designed apps, and now artificial intelligence are capable of communicating with students in ways that more quickly, and more accurately, assess a person's understanding of math.  Why are these tools better?

If a test question involves calculating a final numeric answer, it's often easy for a student to use a trial-and-error approach, such as plugging each multiple-choice answer into the supplied equations to find out which number works.  To defeat this tactic, some multiple-choice test questions include words that really are testing math-based terminology.  Another way to avoid trial-and-error tactics is to ask for an intermediate number, such as the lowest common denominator when adding fractions.  Yet these testing tactics shift the focus to terminology, which is seldom helpful for teaching the underlying concepts of math.

What will replace multiple-choice tests?  Software, including tiny, efficient, math-specific versions of artificial intelligence.  This software will teach and test, doing both at the same time.

Math teachers have a favorite mantra to "show your work."  Well-designed software will follow a student through each step the student takes.  The software will include an on-screen calculator, so using it reveals which concepts are not yet understood.  Adopting that better software will lead to a declining need to assess students using multiple-choice tests.

The future of math education is to use software that integrates that both teaches and tests at the same time.  This approach will waste less time learning how to take multiple-choice tests.  In turn, this efficiency will allow math teachers to better teach the math skills that adults need to learn.

13: Calculus and advanced algebra are for special professions, the mentally adventurous, and the testing industry

Calculus is breathtakingly beautiful.  Using it led to being able to calculate the exact position of a planet at any moment in time.  Integrals allow us to exactly calculate the area under a polynomial curve.  Derivatives allow us to exactly calculate the speed of a falling object at every moment during its fall.

Yet climbing the "advanced-placement" mountain to glimpse the beauty of calculus consumes too much time, effort, and money considering that very very few professions require using calculus for any calculations.

In a similar way, advanced algebra is filled with beautiful concepts that involve vectors, matrices, advanced trigonometry, and imaginary numbers.  Yet those topics also aren't used in hardly any professions.

So why have schools built a well-traveled road to the top of a mountain for a quick view of mathematical beauty?  There are two reasons.

The more noble reason is the hope of training future scientists who will pioneer advances in software, electronics, biochemistry, satellites, and space travel.  And they want to train future engineers who will pioneer new products, better bridges, and faster computers.  These frontiers have become the battlegrounds for nations competing with each other for economic advantages.  That's why lots of tax money is spent on education for training scientists and engineers.

The uglier reason for focusing on seldom-used math concepts is that lots of businesses earn lots of money testing students on the advanced math topics of calculus, advanced algebra, and advanced trigonometry.  Additional businesses profit from preparing students to be successful when they take these college-entrance exams.  And universities can gain prestige and charge higher tuition if more of their graduates enter high-paying professions that involve higher levels of math.

Fortunately the most useful parts of calculus and algebra can be moved into earlier grade levels.  This approach will free up time for high-school students to learn real-life math topics that are currently untaught, yet useful, such as software development using artificial intelligence.
