1: Algebra is the solving of mysteries

Image: young Albert Einstein scratching head in classroom, with other kids in background

The great mathematician Albert Einstein, when he was a child, struggled when he was first learning algebra.  Fortunately a wise uncle advised Albert to think of algebra as a mystery where the purpose is to become a detective and discover the hidden identity of a Mister X.

Image: words: mystery, secret, hidden, clue, puzzle

Imagine the fun children could have if algebra teachers presented mysteries and puzzles with clues, secret knowledge, and hidden insights.

The basics of algebra, starting with addition and subtraction, should be taught at an early age. Specifically, kids should be taught that adding and subtracting the same number to or from both sides of an equation is a useful way to calculate an unknown quantity.  A year or two later they can learn the trick of multiplying and dividing both sides of an equation by the same number.

During this early learning, advanced topics such as polynomials, the order of operations, and when to use capital or lowercase letters as unknown values, must be ignored.

When students reach high school they will not have to be taught the basics for solving an equation to discover an unknown quantity.  Instead they can focus on learning the traditional notation and terminoloy conventions that will be tested on college-entrance exams.  That notation will include the exponential notation, the order of operations, polynomials, logarithms, and other topics most of them will never need after college.

This recommended path will introduce them to negative numbers early.  Those are easy to teach by first limiting math exercises to the numbers negative one, negative two, negative three, negative four, and negative five.  They can encounter these simple negative numbers on a printed-on-paper ruler that extends from negative five on the left to positive five on the right, with zero in the middle.  They will learn that adding positive integers amounts to counting to the right, subtracting positive integers amounts to counting to the left, and the counting can temporarily involve the negative integers to the left of zero.  One year later they will be ready to learn the math shortcut of adding and subtracting negative numbers.

In hindsight it will become clear that what teachers have been referring to as math "problems" are 
really exercises, tasks, and puzzles.

Real-life problems, the mystery-solving kind, require practice learning to look at clues, discover patterns and insights, and become comfortable with not excessively relying on following step-by-step instructions.

The skill of learning how to solve a mystery has always been valuable.  It's the basis for solving real-life problems.

These real-life problem-solving skills have become even more important now that computer software is easily trained to "solve" math exercise, math tasks, and math puzzles using "perfect" algebra notation.

Throughout the last couple of centuries countless students have correctly guessed they were being taught excessively rigorous details that aren't especially valuable for solving the real-life math problems they will to encounter as an adult.  Especially because they notice their parents don't remember the same details from their school years.

Algebra should be used to teach students how approach simplified real-life math challenges with a spirit of adventure, fascination, determination, and insightful thinking.  This mystery-solving spirit will better prepare young people for solving real-life challenges.

Plus it will provide job security because it's the kind of thinking that artificial intelligence cannot easily mimic.

2: Division is everywhere, yet hidden

Do you get caught into the trap of thinking about division as if it's about cutting a whole pizza into equal-size slices?  And sorting objects into equal-size piles?  And dealing with fractions?

Image: pizza slices, equal-size piles, fractions

If so, your math teachers failed to explain that division is a tool that has many different uses.

As an analogy, the excessive focus on division as a tool for cutting is like giving someone a multi-tool or swiss army knife and only showing them how to use the knife blade.  The many other fold-out tools within the multi-tool are getting ignored.

So here's the next big secret.  When you get stumped by a real-life math problem, solving the math probably involves division.

Why division?  If the math just involves addition and subtraction, the solution is obvious.  This is basically just accounting.  If the math just involves multiplication, that's just a shortcut for counting using big steps, so it's easy to understand.

In contrast, division hides under many disguises and aliases.

Here are some words that hide specific uses of division: percent, ratio, proportion, rate, reciprocals, and the word "per."  can be thought of as a special kind of use for division.

The failure of schools to fully explore these deeper uses of division is why later topics such as statistics, trigonometry, chemistry, and physics become much harder than necessary.

One of the overlooked uses of division is to compare things.

If someone says it took them 20 minutes to travel five miles, and another person says it took them 15 minutes to travel seven miles, how much traffic did each of them encounter?  The standard way to compare these cases is to calculate miles per hour.  The first person traveled an average of ... miles per hours, and the second person traveled an average of ... miles per hour.

3: Fractions are for communication, not calculation
Images: ancient Romans

If you ask someone how much of a beverage to pour into their glass, a reply of "two thirds full" is quite clear.  In contrast, it would be confusing to refer to filling a beverage glass to "sixty six percent full."

If someone asks you how much soup is left in the fridge, saying "half of it is left" is a clear answer.  But saying "zero point five of the soup remains" would be silly.

Clearly, fractions are useful for communication.

However, fractions are not useful when doing calculations.  In that case, a fraction is a division task that has not yet been done.

Suppose you have a distance measurement of three quarters of an inch and another distance measurement of five eighths of an inch, and you want to add them.  You grab a calculator and divide three by four to get zero point seven five.  Then you divide five by eight to get zero point six two five.  Then you add those two decimal numbers to get the sum, which is one point three seven five.  Notice the first step is to do the division calculation that was being avoided.

Why do schools waste so much time teaching students how to add and subtract fractions?

Centuries ago ancient people didn't know how to divide three by four.  So they left the fraction of three fourths as a fraction.  They wrote down the fraction as if it was a final number.  They developed clever tricks that sometimes made it possible to add and subtract fractions and yield a useful simpler number.

But now, students soon discover that adding and subtracting fractions is not useful in the modern world.  When they do math using a spreadsheet app, they discover fractions cannot be typed into a spreadsheet cell.  And they never see a fraction in any calculated result.  Students feel betrayed.  They also begin to distrust teachers for also teaching other math skills that have become obsolete.

Cursive writing has been removed from school lessons because it's obsolete.  Now it's time to remove the obsolete skill of looking for common denominators in numbers expressed as fractions.

When numbers are expressed as a fraction, that's just a division task that hasn't yet been done.

When a student learns algebra they do need to learn some of the numerically obsolete clever tricks, such as dividing the top and bottom of a fraction by the same unknown quantity.  At that time they can be reminded the same trick works with known numbers.  But math educators need to stop pretending that numeric fractions should be simplified instead of simply doing the division represented by the fraction.

We can foster joy by explaining a few of the clever tricks learned centuries ago for adding and subtracting fractions.  But that appreciation is for an historic relic, not an attempt to preserve an obsolete skill.

When you see or hear a fraction with specific numbers, recognize it's a useful way to communicate a quantity.  But if you need to use that fraction for any calcuation, the first step is to do the division calculation before you combine the result with other numbers.

4: Decimal inches are superior to fractional inches

Possibly the biggest loss of joy in math education happens when young students learn to use a ruler marked in halves, quarters, eighths, and possibly sixteenths of an inch.

Splitting an inch into halves, quarters, and eighths has been obsolete for at least a few centuries.  Yet students are required to buy rulers with these obsolete markings.

Why do schools waste time teaching students how to read fractions from a ruler as if inches can only be measured using fractions?

There are still a very few professions and hobbies that use fractional inches.  Sewing is one example.  Yet when cloth tape measures become available in both decimal inches and fractional inches, that industry will quickly change.  Each time a calculation is needed, it will be easy to look at the measurement on the fractional scale and convert it to the decimal scale, and the decimal measurements can be used for all calculations.

The same is true for construction projects.  If you do construction as a hobby, buy a "decimal tape measure" which is sometimes called an "engineering" tape measure.  Adding and subtracting decimal inches is a joy compared to dealing with fractional inches.

Schools should be asking students to buy a ruler that shows decimal inches.  Ideally it also should show centimeters, which are always divided into ten intervals, never eigth or sixteen intervals.

Also schools should ask students to buy a ruler that goes up to ten inches, not twelve.  Those ten inches provide a useful way to visualize percentages.  That's because each tic mark is one percent of the ruler's total marked length.

Decimal numbers are long overdue.  We don't need to switch to the metric system to gain the advantage of using decimal numbers.  We do need to stop teaching fractions as if they still deserve to be used for distance measurements.

5: Units, pick one in each category when calculating

Europeans use the metric system, which measures time in seconds.  Yet two European friends don't say "Bye, see you in 605 kiloseconds" instead of saying "Bye, see you in a week."  (In case you care, a week is actually 604.8 kiloseconds.)  Everyone, worldwide, speaks using the same familiar time units we use in the US, namely minutes, hours, days, weeks, months, and years.

Some people think we should adopt metric units in the kitchen for food prep.  Yet that misses the point.  Recipes can continue to specify quantities in tablespoons, teaspoons, and cups.  And we can continue to conveniently specify other food quantities in pints, quarts, and liters.  And we can continue to use yards for football fields, and feet for cutting lumber, and inches for smaller sizes.

Only when we need to do calculations do we need to choose just one unit for each measurement category.

This concept is similar to the distinction between fractions and decimal numbers.  Fractions are fine for communication, but decimals are needed for calculations.  In a similar way, any unit can be used for communication, but choosing just one unit per category is necessary when doing calculations.

What are the unit categories?  Distance, time, and weight are the main categories.  Areas could be measured using squared distances such as square feet.  Volumes could be measured using cubic distances such as cubic inches.  Yet even the metric system measures volume using liters instead of using cubic meters.  So the category of volume in the kitchen is where we use teaspoons, cups, pints, and so on.

Although scientists promote the idea of switching to the metric system, they overlook the fact that seconds are not useful for specifying a day or a week or a year.

Some people will promote the idea of using the metric system for kitchen measurements of volume.  Ironically Europeans measure food quantities using a weight scale, which is at least party because cubic millimeters and cubic centimeters have their own complications.  Specifically, although ten millimeters equals one centimeter, one thousand cubic millimeters equals one cubic centimeter.

The complication with volume becomes clear when a baker mixes one teaspoon of salt with two cups of flour.  That's a ratio of ....  Cheap plastic measuring scoops easily handle this dramatic range of volumes.  Doing the equivalent using a digital weight scale requires an expensive precise electronic scale.

Different math situations require different units.  When a reporter asks how long it will take a spacecraft to reach the moon, the scientific answer expressed in seconds isn't useful.  Instead it needs to be communicated in days, not seconds.

When communicating, pick a unit that fits the situation.  When calculating, convert each number into the same specific unit in each category.  Then, if needed, convert the calculated result back into a meaningful unit for communication.

7: History of math enhances understanding math

Ancient Romans had to learn clever tricks to add and subtract fractions.  The best-known of these tricks is to use the "lowest common denominator" for the bottom of two fractions being added or subtracted.  Why didn't they just do the division implied by the fraction.

They used Roman numerals.  So a fraction such as .... would be expressed as ....  Even if the result was going to be an integer (whole number), that division task would be difficult.  If they saw both the number at the top and bottom of the fraction were both even numbers, they divided both numbers by two, which was much easier to do.

Knowing about this difficulty helps us appreciate why fractions were used as numeric results.  And it helps us appreciate why it was useful to learn clever tricks for adding and subtracting fractions.  Now that we use the decimal number system instead of Romand numerals, we no longer need to learn those clever tricks.

Why?  .... bank accounting between cities by separating debit and credit calculations among different employees, safer travel with less highway robbery because transporting coins and jewels less often needed.

Why were logarithms especially useful for a couple of centuries?  ....  logarithms tables ... slide rule ... mechanical hand-cranked calculators

Later it was done on paper using quill pens, which frequently had to have ink added from an inkwell.  Limiting the use of paretheses was helpful. ... Students deserve to learn about math history.  It's fascinating.

8: Terminology is much less important than concepts

When scientists work on solving a math challenge, rarely do they think in words such as "here's a variable" and "here's the unknown quantity."  When engineers design a bridge that uses triangles for stability, they don't name the trianges as "isocoles" or "right triangle."  And only a few people remember the name "Pythagorean theorem" when they remember that, for a right triangle, the sum of the squares of the two shorter sides equals the square of the longer side.

So why do math textbooks and standardized tests put so much emphasis on terminology?

A few decades ago, before videos and audio recordings became inexpensive to produce and play back, knowledge was written in books.  An easy way to find out if a student had read a chapter in a textbook was to ask questions about the definitions of terms defined in the chapter.

For this reason, textbook authors who were experts in their academic fields, were told to include terminology that they, the experts, did not themselves use in their own academic field.

Alas, public education, including math education, now waste excessive amounts of textbook space and classroom time teaching terminology with carefully worded definitions that won't be useful in the real worlds of business and government.

Instead it's the concepts behind those words that are important to learn.

Earlier you learned that division is hidden in words such as ratio, proportion, rate, and reciprocals.  Yet those words are not important for solving real challenges as long as the underlying concepts are clearly understood.

Of course terminology becomes important when communicating through words.  However, the internet, Wikipedia, and AI services can quickly supply the correct term, or the definition of a term, when those terms and definitions are needed.

Math education should reduce the number of math terms being taught in school.  Let's update video and audio learning resources so they don't make the same mistake as textbooks, namely an excessive focus on terminology and not enough focus on clearly understanding the underlying concepts.

9:Testing math distracts from teaching math

Multiple choice tests are obsolete.  Yet high schools spend lots of time teaching students how to do well on multiple choice tests.  Wealthy parents spend lots of money on experts who teach students how to get higher scores on multiple choice tests.  And universities heavily rely on multiple-choice test scores when deciding who to admit.

Computers, smart phones, well-designed apps, and now artificial intelligence are capable of communicating with students in ways that more quickly, and more accurately, assess a person's understanding of math.  Why are these tools better?

If a test question involves calculating a final numeric answer, it's often easy for a student to use a trial-and-error approach, such as plugging each multiple-choice answer into the supplied equations to find out which number works.  To defeat this tactic, some multiple-choice test questions include words that really are testing math-based terminology.  Another way to avoid trial-and-error tactics is to ask for an intermediate number, such as the lowest common denominator when adding fractions.  Yet these testing tactics shift the focus to terminology, which is seldom helpful for teaching the underlying concepts of math.

What will replace multiple-choice tests?  Software, including tiny, efficient, math-specific versions of artificial intelligence.  This software will teach and test, doing both at the same time.

Math teachers have a favorite mantra to "show your work."  Well-designed software will follow a student through each step the student takes.  The software will include an on-screen calculator, so using it reveals which concepts are not yet understood.  Adopting that better software will lead to a declining need to assess students using multiple-choice tests.

The future of math education is to use software that integrates that both teaches and tests at the same time.  This approach will waste less time learning how to take multiple-choice tests.  In turn, this efficiency will allow math teachers to better teach the math skills that adults need to learn.

13: Calculus and advanced algebra are for special professions, the mentally adventurous, and the testing industry

Calculus is breathtakingly beautiful.  Using it led to being able to calculate the exact position of a planet at any moment in time.  Integrals allow us to exactly calculate the area under a polynomial curve.  Derivatives allow us to exactly calculate the speed of a falling object at every moment during its fall.

Yet climbing the "advanced-placement" mountain to glimpse the beauty of calculus consumes too much time, effort, and money considering that very very few professions require using calculus for any calculations.

In a similar way, advanced algebra is filled with beautiful concepts that involve vectors, matrices, advanced trigonometry, and imaginary numbers.  Yet those topics also aren't used in hardly any professions.

So why have schools built a well-traveled road to the top of a mountain for a quick view of mathematical beauty?  There are two reasons.

The more noble reason is the hope of training future scientists who will pioneer advances in software, electronics, biochemistry, satellites, and space travel.  And they want to train future engineers who will pioneer new products, better bridges, and faster computers.  These frontiers have become the battlegrounds for nations competing with each other for economic advantages.  That's why lots of tax money is spent on education for training scientists and engineers.

The uglier reason for focusing on seldom-used math concepts is that lots of businesses earn lots of money testing students on the advanced math topics of calculus, advanced algebra, and advanced trigonometry.  Additional businesses profit from preparing students to be successful when they take these college-entrance exams.  And universities can gain prestige and charge higher tuition if more of their graduates enter high-paying professions that involve higher levels of math.

Fortunately the most useful parts of calculus and algebra can be moved into earlier grade levels.  This approach will free up time for high-school students to learn real-life math topics that are currently untaught, yet useful, such as software development using artificial intelligence.
