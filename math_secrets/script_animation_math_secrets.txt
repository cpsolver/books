section-1-details-begin
1: Algebra is the solving of mysteries
Images: kids with magnifying glass
Images, words: mystery, secret, hidden, clue, puzzle

The great mathematician Albert Einstein, when he was a child, struggled when he was first learning algebra.  Fortunately a wise uncle advised Albert to think of algebra as a mystery where the purpose is to become a detective and discover the hidden identity of a Mister X.

Imagine the fun children could have if algebra teachers presented mysteries and puzzles with clues, hidden insights, and secret knowledge.  This can be done at an early age, starting with just using addition and subtraction.

When a child enters high school they can finally focus on learning the parts of algebra that involve polynomials, which involve squaring a number and cubing a number.  Most student won't ever need to use polynomials after they graduate college, so let's delay that part of algebra until just before college.

Critics of this approach will warn that students won't be ready to understand negative numbers that can arise when this joyous mystery-solving approach is used.  Nonsense.  Young children can quickly learn about negative numbers by limiting them to negative one to negative five, and giving children a printed-on-paper ruler that extends from negative five on the left to positive five on the right, with zero in the middle.  Simply teach them that adding positive integers amounts to counting to the right, subtracting positive integers amounts to counting to the left, and the counting can temporarily involve the negative integers to the left of zero.  One year later they will be ready to learn about adding and subtracting negative numbers.

Eventually it will become clear that what teachers refer to as "math problems" are really math exercises and math tasks.  The current tradition of only teaching students how to follow well-defined steps is not what real problem-solving is about.

Real-life problems, the mystery-solving kind, require practice learning to look at clues, discover patterns, and become comfortable with moments of not knowing what to do next.

2: Division is everywhere, yet hidden

The next big secret is that when you get stumped by a real-life math problem, solving the math probably involves division.  In other words, students should be taught that when they become a detective for solving a puzzling math mystery, the first thing to guess is that solving the problem probably involves division.  If division isn't involved, a math problem probably just involves addition and subtraction, and everyone understands how addition and subtraction work.

Do you get caught into the trap of thinking about division as if it's about cutting a whole pizza into equal-size slices?  And sorting objects into equal-size piles?  And dealing with fractions?  If so, your math teachers failed to explain the "why" behind division.

Division is like an iceberg.  The tip is easy to see.  Yet beneath the surface you will discover hidden secret disguises for division.  Specifcially, here are some words that hide specific uses of division: percent, ratio, proportion, rate, reciprocals, and the word "per."  can be thought of as a special kind of use for division.

Alas, schools fail to explore these deeper uses of division.  This failure is why later topics such as statistics, trigonometry, chemistry, and physics become harder than necessary.

One of the overlooked uses of division is to compare things.  If someone says it took them 20 minutes to travel five miles, and another person says it took them 15 minutes to travel seven miles, how much traffic did each of them encounter?  The standard way to compare these cases is to calculate miles per hour.  The first person traveled an average of ... miles per hours, and the second person traveled an average of ... miles per hour.  That's one use of division.

5: Units, pick one in each category when calculating

Europeans use the metric system, which measures time in seconds.  Yet two European friends don't say "Bye, see you in 605 kiloseconds" instead of saying "Bye, see you in a week."  (In case you care, a week is actually 604.8 kiloseconds.)  Everyone, worldwide, speaks using the same familiar time units we use in the US, namely minutes, hours, days, weeks, months, and years.

Some people think we should adopt metric units in the kitchen for food prep.  Yet that misses the point.  Recipies can continue to specify quantities in tablespoons, teaspoons, and cups.  And we can continue to conveniently specify other food quantities in pints, quarts, and liters.  And we can continue to use yards for football fields, and feet for cutting lumber, and inches for smaller sizes.

Only when we need to do calculations do we need to choose just one unit for each measurement category.

This concept is similar to the distinction between fractions and decimal numbers.  Fractions are fine for communication, but decimals are needed for calculations.  In a similar way, any unit can be used for communication, but choosing just one unit per category is necessary when doing calculations.

What are the unit categories?  Distance, time, and weight are the main categories.  Areas could be measured using squared distances such as square feet.  Volumes could be measured using cubic distances such as cubic inches.  Yet even the metric system measures volume using liters instead of using cubic meters.  So the category of volume in the kitchen is where we use teaspoons, cups, pints, and so on.

13: Calculus and advanced algebra are for special professions, the mentally adventurous, and the testing industry

Calculus is breathtakingly beautiful.  Using it leads has led to being able to calculate the exact position of a planet at any moment in time.  Integrals allow us to exactly calculate the area under a polynomial curve.  Derivatives allow us to exactly calculate the speed of a falling object at every moment during its fall.

Yet climbing the "advanced-placement" mountain to glimpse the beauty of calculus consumes too much time, effort, and money considering that very very few professions require using calculus for any calculations.

In a similar way, advanced algebra is filled with beautiful concepts that involve vectors, matrices, advanced trigonometry, and imaginary numbers.  Yet those topics also aren't used in hardly any professions.

So why have schools built a well-traveled road to the top of a mountain for a quick view of mathematical beauty?  There are two reasons.

The more noble reason is the hope of training future scientists who will pioneer advances in electronics, satellites, and space travel.  And they want to train future engineers who will pioneer new products, better bridges, and faster computers.  These frontiers have become the battlegrounds for nations competing with each other for economic advantages.  That's why lots of tax money is spent on education for training scientists and engineers.

The uglier reason is that lots of businesses earn lots of money testing students on the advanced math topics of calculus, advanced algebra, and advanced trigonometry.  Additional businesses profit from preparing students to be successful when they take these college-entrance exams.  And universities can gain prestige and charge higher tuition if more of their graduates enter high-paying professions that involve higher levels of math.
